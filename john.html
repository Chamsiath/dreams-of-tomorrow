John, the Aspiring Pilot ✈️

“Bye, Mama!” said young John cheerfully.
“Bye…” his mother replied softly, unaware that it would be the last time she would ever see her son.
A tragic airplane crash occurred in Oklahoma that day. Everyone on board died, except for one pilot. Among the victims was John’s mother, Rebecca.
Years later, John woke up drenched in sweat, the same nightmare haunting him again. It was always that last goodbye, that last smile, that last sight. He glanced at the framed photo of his mother beside his bed, then turned it face down on the table, unable to look at it.
As he fell back asleep, he dreamt of her once more.
…Morning arrived…
John went to the dining room.
“Good morning, John!” said his father as he was making omelets for his son.
“Dad, I’m 18. I can make omelets on my own,” remarked John as he began washing the plates.
His father, Kai, pinched his cheek. “You know, in Hawaiian, when someone annoys us we say…”
Then both of them said in unison, “Kulikuli!” and started laughing.
“Aren’t you late for… what do we call it in English again? Um—Kulanui!” said his father.
“It’s not called ‘Kulanui,’ Dad; that’s Hawaiian. It’s called university,” said John.
“Oh yes… So are you late?” asked the father.
“No,” replied John. “Actually, Dad, I’m early,” he said as he grabbed his toast and omelet and headed off to university.
His academy was called Flight Academy of Skys, founded by Captain Skys, a skilled pilot. John was studying aeronautical engineering and pilot training.
He sat down in class, and when the lesson began, the teacher entered and closed the door.
“Anyone who is late shall not enter this class until the end,” declared the professor.
He came to the center of the room, faced the students, and said, “Welcome to university, students! I am your physics professor, Dr. Gilbert Alexander. It is a pleasure to make your acquaintance. We shall commence with Unit 1 of Physics—Kinematics. So, what is kinematics…?”
The professor continued as John opened his notebook and began writing.
…Break time in the canteen…
“Um, hello?” someone said, making John turn around.
“Do we know each other?” asked John in confusion.
“Yes, we do! I’m William De LaChaise. We were friends in primary school,” said William.
“Oh! William. How are you doing?” John asked as they sat down to talk.
“Fine. So, I'm just curious… Why are you in a flight school? Didn’t you say you wanted to be a doctor?” asked William, making John chuckle.
“True, I wanted to be a doctor. But you know, as you grow up, your goals change,” said John, his mind reminiscing the news of the airplane crash in Oklahoma.
“The 2012 Oklahoma airplane crash,” said John as he looked at William. “Do you know about it?”
“Yes, I do. The one where 100 passengers and one pilot died, with only a severely injured pilot who survived,” said William as he sipped his tea.
“Among them…” John said as he looked toward the sky, “was my mother.”
William paused momentarily.
“I want to be a pilot. I want to inspire and lead like my mother. My mother, Rebecca, was a retired pilot. Her motto was, ‘If you ever get lost, look at the skies.’” John smiled softly, then looked back at William.
“So, I shall look at the skies for as long as it takes until I reach my dream,” said John with passion.
“Anyway, what do you want to be?” asked John.
“I want to be a military pilot,” said William.
“Oh, okay—” said John as the bell rang.
After John’s lectures, he headed off to his part-time job as a waiter in a chic restaurant called La Rose. The staff always entered through the back door. He went to the staff restroom, combed his hair neatly, straightened his uniform, and stepped into the bustling kitchen.
“Good evening, Steve,” said John to the chef.
“Good evening, John. Looking sharp as always,” Steve replied with a wink as he cut cucumbers with great speed.
“You know it,” John said with a grin as he dusted his attire.
A waiter handed Steve a list of orders. Steve clapped his hands to get everyone’s attention.
“Alright, listen up, chefs! Tonight marks the anniversary of La Rose. The founder himself will be dining with us for the first time in ten years! Let’s make sure tonight’s dishes are unforgettable.”
“Aye, aye, sir!” the chefs chorused.
John chuckled.
“They sound like a pirate crew,” said Sasha, one of the waitresses carrying a plate of ice-creams on one hand.
“Indeed, what a joyful crew,” John replied, smiling as he stepped into the main restaurant.
“Waiter, waiter!” a voice called from across the room.
“Yes, sir?” John replied, walking toward the table.
“Good evening, sirs and mademoiselles,” John greeted elegantly and he leaned closer to the table.
“What a gentleman,” one of the ladies at the table said with a giggle.
John froze.
“John, do you remember me? Mark Quitton, your classmate in high school,” said Mark.
John’s eyes widened. It was him, Mark Quitton, his high school bully. John began reminiscing about the memories of soaked textbooks, cruel laughter, hands around his neck, as a quick flash in his mind. He swallowed hard in anxiousness but forced a polite smile.
“How may I help you this lovely evening?” he asked, steadying his voice.
“We’d like two plates of Beef Wellington and five chocolate soufflés,” Mark said casually.
“Duly noted,” John replied, jotting it down before hurrying back to the kitchen as beads of sweat began forming on his forehead.
Inside, his hands trembled slightly as he passed the order slip to Steve.
“You okay, kid?” Steve asked, noticing his pale expression.
“Yeah… just tired,” John muttered.
Hours passed. John served tables, refilled drinks, and smiled through exhaustion. When he returned to Mark’s table to deliver the dishes, Mark smirked.
“So, the kid who used to run from me serves food now? Life’s funny, huh?” he sneered.
John took a slow breath. “Yes,” he said calmly, “life is funny… it also gives second chances.”
Mark frowned, caught off guard. John placed the plates down gracefully, bowed slightly, and walked away.
“Enjoy your meal,” said John.
By the time the restaurant closed, John was the last waiter to leave. He locked up the place and gave the key to the guardian. As he walked home, he looked up at the night sky and smiled softly.
“Mom,” he whispered, “I didn’t run this time…and I wish to never run again.”
The following week, his Flight Simulator Training exams began. The sunlight broke through his window. John rubbed his eyes, stretched a bit, and looked at the photo of his mother again as he sighed. This time around, he didn’t turn it face down.
“Good morning, Mom,” he said with a small smile. “Another day closer to the skies.”
At the academy, he walked into the simulator room. It was a big space filled with blinking panels and the smell of machine oil. He saw a man with blue eyes and blonde hair in a military uniform.
“Good morning, students!” the man said.
“Good morning, sir!” the students replied.
“Great! I’m your instructor, Captain Hayes. Today, we will practice your flying skills using a simulator,” Captain Hayes said.
“Yes, sir!” the students answered in unison.
“As the saying goes, when everything seems to be against you…” Captain Hayes began.
“Um…” a student murmured.
“When everything seems to be against you, remember that an airplane takes off against the wind, not with it. Repeat!” the Captain instructed.
“When everything seems to be against you, remember that an airplane takes off against the wind, not with it!” the students echoed.
“By Henry Ford. Okay! Good!” said Captain Hayes.
“You!” the captain called, pointing at John.
“Yes, sir!” John replied, standing tall.
“Get in the pilot simulator seat and show us what you can do!” ordered Captain Hayes.
“Sir, yes, sir!” John said.
As he sat in the pilot’s seat, his hands shook slightly. Captain Hayes noticed.
“Nervous, boy?” the captain asked.
John nodded lightly. “A little.”
“Good!” the captain grinned. “That shows you care about what you’re doing. Good luck!”
John put on the virtual reality headset, and the simulator came to life. The virtual sky stretched out like a huge blue expanse that almost felt real. He took a deep breath, grabbed the control stick, and whispered, “For you, Mom.”
The simulator exercise continued. Clouds flew by, and virtual winds tossed the plane.
“Steady, John… steady,” he whispered to himself.
Minutes later, the simulator announced the final checkpoint. John prepared the plane to land.
“Almost there… almost—”
Suddenly, a red alert flashed; it was the simulator engine. John panicked and pulled the controls too sharply. The plane veered and crashed.
“Failed,” the simulator voice declared.
John felt his heart sink. His dream of flying smoothly and passing the first try had vanished. He removed the headset and ran his fingers through his hair, trying to hide his frustration as Captain Hayes approached.
“John, failure is part of learning! Your first mistake doesn’t define who you are. It shows you what to improve. You’ll get it next time!”
John nodded, swallowing hard. As he passed a small table in the corner, he noticed a dusty, leather-bound journal titled: Rebecca’s Flight Notes. He gasped.
He opened the journal. Each entry was filled with advice, drawings of planes, tips for handling turbulence, and reflections. He was amazed.
"Is this for mom?" John wondered.
Captain Hayes walked up to John calmly. “I knew your mother, Rebecca,” he said quietly with a smile.
John’s eyes widened. “You… you did?!”
Hayes nodded. “She was an exceptional pilot and co-pilot,” he continued. “You have her courage in you. This journal is the last thing I have of her. Although you touched it without permission, I want you to have it. Use it as inspiration to fly higher,” Captain Hayes said, giving John a gentle tap on the back before walking away.
“Tomorrow,” John murmured, “I’ll do better. I’ll fly… I’ll reach the skies,” he said, holding the book close.
After the simulator session, Captain Hayes gathered everyone.
“The next simulator session will be in a week,” he announced.
As dusk arrived, John started walking home with notes in his hand, determined to face the skies again.
“I don't know exactly how I will reach the skies, but I am hopeful. Now that I finally have my engine fuel,” he said, looking at his mother’s notes, “I believe it is more than possible.”
When he got home, John went to work. He began watching pilot tutorials, studying physics, and memorizing flight procedures.
“This is my first year in this flight academy. Let’s do this!” John said to motivate himself.
Days turned into nights, and evenings turned into mornings. John studied tirelessly, sometimes falling asleep at his desk next to his mother’s photo. The week passed quickly until Monday, the day of the simulator exam.
“All right, students!” said Captain Hayes. “Today, you’ll prove you have what it takes to conquer the skies.”
John’s palms were sweaty, but he felt hopeful. He climbed into the simulator again, the familiar sound of the virtual plane filling his ears.
“Be calm,” he whispered to himself.
The virtual plane took off smoothly and steadily. When the simulation ended, Captain Hayes nodded with pride.
“Well done, Cadet Kalama. You’ve earned your first wings.”
Tears ran down John’s cheeks.
“I did it,” he said through his tears. “I got my first wings.”
“I did it, Mom,” he thought to himself.
“Hey hey hey! Man up! Stop crying in front of the ladies,” Captain Hayes said, giving John a friendly nudge as the ladies chuckled.
John wiped his tears, straightened up, and replied, “Sir, yes, sir!”
Three Years Later…
The auditorium buzzed with uniforms, chatter, and excitement. Afternoon had arrived at the Flight Academy of Skys. The graduates stood in line, with shining badges on their clothing.
Captain Hayes stood at the podium, grinning as he faced the students.
“Today, we celebrate not just your success,” he said, “but your persistence, the courage to keep flying even when the wind was against you.”
John stood among his fellow cadets, smiling with a sense of achievement. He had finally made it. After all the sleepless nights, failures, and memories of his mother, he had risen.
When his name was called, he stepped forward to receive his pilot certificate.
“John Kalama,” the announcer said, “for exceptional performance, resilience, and discipline in flight simulation and aeronautical training.”
John shook Captain Hayes’s hand. “You’ve made your mother proud, son,” the captain said with a small smile.
“Thank you, sir,” John replied, his voice shaking a little.
After the ceremony, students tossed their caps in the air and took a picture. After that, John walked to the open field next to the academy. The air smelled like jet fuel and fresh grass. He looked up at the clouds as a gentle breeze brushed across his face. For a short moment, he felt as if she were right there smiling.
"Mom?" he said, turning his back. When he saw no one he faced front, "Must be my imagination," he thought to himself as he chuckled.
He glanced down at his certificate, then back to the endless sky.
“This isn’t the end,” he said, squeezing his certificate, “It’s just the beginning.”
He turned toward the hangar, where a single small plane waited for him. It was time for his first real flight test.
John climbed into the cockpit, his heart pounding.
The engine started. As the wheels lifted off the ground, he grinned and said, “For you, Mom. Always.”
The plane climbed into the open blue.
Two Years Later…
The intercom buzzed softly.
“Hello, ladies and gentlemen,” came a calm, confident voice. “This is your regional pilot, John Kalama. Please fasten your seatbelts as we are ready for takeoff.”
John smiled behind the cockpit controls.
He glanced at a small photograph taped near the throttle, showing his mother’s smiling face as she boarded a plane.
“If you ever get lost, look at the skies,” he whispered, remembering the words she used to tell him while fixing his clothes.
The engines roared to life. John’s heart beat steadily, not from fear this time, but from fulfillment. As the plane lifted off, the clouds stretched endlessly before him in the warm glow of dusk.
He smiled softly.
“I am finally home. In the skies,” said John.

 “The journey of a thousand miles begins with a single step.” -Lao Tzu
