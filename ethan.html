🪄Ethan, the Veil of Elsmore ⋆⭒˚｡⋆

There once lived a boy called Alan. Alan lived in the quiet, mystical village of Elsmore, nestled between misty forests and sparkling pixie rivers. The village was small– stone cottages, magicians, chimneys bringing out smoke, the usual.
Birds sang in the morning, and the soft splashing of the river during the afternoon, the villagers going to sleep at night. Alan learned all sorts of things in Elsmore: washing clothes in the river, sweeping the stone paths, baking bread with Mrs. Thistle, and riding the village horses that roamed freely.
But Elsmore was no ordinary village. Every night, when the people of Elsmore went to sleep, a creature known as the Veil was said to roam the shadows. It was believed the Veil had once been a man, cursed for his greed, arrogance, and pride, doomed to haunt the village forever.
“The Veil is no friendly creature,” said Alan’s grandmother, Gladys, her voice trembling slightly. “He has fangs sharper than knives, a breath fouler than the rottenest ponds, a hideous face, and most of all—”
“Ohhh…” the children gasped as they came closer to Gladys, wide-eyed.
“Most of all,” she continued, “the Veil does not like light or mirrors.”
“Granny, granny! How do we know when the Veil is near?” asked one child, her small hands holding the edge of her blanket tightly.
“When you hear the forest whisper,” Gladys replied, mysteriously,
“Ohhh…” the children gasped again.
At that moment, the trees outside began swaying, the branches rustled, the forest… whispered. Gladys’ eyes widened. She grabbed a bucket, poured water over the fireplace, and hurried the children to their beds.
But Alan couldn’t sleep, he was curious and adventurous. He wanted to see the Veil for himself. He quietly slipped out of bed, tiptoed to the living room, grabbed a stick, and pushed the door open. A soft mist swirled as the forest went on whispering.
Something landed on his shoulder. Alan screamed in alarm and rushed into the forest, not looking back, until he reached the Pixie Pond. In his panic, he tripped on a tree root and fell face-first. When he looked up, the Veil stood before him, shrouded in shadow, his fangs glinting faintly in the moonlight, a stench of decay filling the air.
Alan covered his nose and held his stick tightly. The Veil stared into the water, seeing his own reflection, and shrieked in sorrow. Then, he looked at Alan.
“What do you want? Go back home. Be kind to your friends and family. Be generous, polite, and humble,” the Veil growled.
Alan swallowed, his voice trembling. “If you have learned from your mistake, then you can change.”
“It’s too late for me,” the Veil whispered. “I am cursed and will never be a man again. I was handsome, wealthy… I had everything. Now, I have nothing.”
Alan felt sad for the creature. The moon once covered by the clouds, reflected on the pond, suddenly turning red. Alan, confused, looked at the Veil only to see his red eyes, sharp as a cat’s. He looked around as wolves howled in the distance, then back to the Veil.
“Sweet dreams, Alan…” the Veil whispered.
Alan fainted, clutching his stick.
The next morning, Alan recounted his encounter to the villagers. Nobody believed him. They murmured nervously about the Veil, calling it legend, but Alan knew the truth. That night, when the forest whispered again, he felt a stronger urge to see the Veil.
“I have to help him,” Alan whispered to himself. “If he can change, maybe he can become… a human again.”
He prepared carefully: a satchel with bread, a small lantern, and a thick rope. This was no longer curiosity but rather it was courage, and a decision that would lead him far beyond the village paths he knew.
Alan followed the whispers into the forest, deeper than he had ever gone. Mist swirled around him as strange noises echoed through the trees, and glowing eyes watched from the shadows. The Veil appeared beside him, towering and grim.
“Why are you here?” the Veil asked, his voice rough.
“I… I want to help you,” Alan said firmly. “You don’t have to be like this forever.”
“Young Alan, I have already told you there is no hope for me,” said the Veil, looking at the pixies that surrounded the lake as he stepped back.
“Oh, yes! The Veil does not like light,” thought Alan, watching the Veil move closer to the dark forest shadows.
“There is hope! As long as there’s life, there’s hope!” shouted Alan.
The Veil smirked, his crooked front teeth and canines sharp. “You really are a funny boy, Alan.” He then drew his hand from his clothing, though Alan only saw his sharp black nails. The Veil removed his gloves, and touched a flower. The flower blackened, turned to dark dust, and disappeared as the wind blew.
“This is cataclysm. Everything I touch turns to ash. Only my clothing and hood are exceptions,” the Veil said. “So, young boy, are you sure you still want to help me regain my humanity?”
“Yes…” Alan said, gulping.
“What a courageous fellow you are,” the Veil said, snapping his fingers, making a baby dragon appear. He slipped his glove back on, petted the dragon, then looked at Alan and said, “He’s Alan. You are his rider, okay?”
The dragon let out a small roar, its wide, cute eyes sparkling.
“Alan, hop on. You’ve made me determined to get back my humanity,” said the Veil, smirking as he began to float.
Alan climbed onto the dragon, and they flew away.
The two began journeying together.
“So… who cursed you?” Alan asked.
“A witch,” the Veil answered shortly.
The wind rushed past Alan’s face as he clung tightly to the dragon. Below, the twisted forest stretched endlessly, shadows shifting like living things. The Veil floated beside him, a dark, silent figure, his hood pulled low, red eyes dark and sinister.
Alan swallowed hard, heart pounding. “Where… where are we going?” he shouted over the susurrus of the wind.
The Veil’s voice was calm but distant. “First, we must reach the Whispering Cliffs. After, the swamp of the Witch. The witch awaits beyond them. But beware… the forest does not welcome the curious.”
The dragon let out a small squeak as it navigated through low-hanging branches. Alan ducked, narrowly avoiding sharp leaves and snapping twigs. Every time he tried to look closer at the Veil, the Veil shifted further into the shadows of the forest, keeping a careful distance. Alan noticed faint cracks in the Veil’s armor of pride.
They passed a river, a marshy, rainfed forest. The current being very strong, made the dragon wobble. Alan gritted his teeth, gripping its scales. “Hold on!” the Veil called, floating beside them, hand hovering above the water.
Suddenly, glowing, strange, twisted creatures of the forest appeared.
“The Whispering Cliffs guardians,” said the Veil as his fingers twitched. Alan froze, the baby dragon let out a small, frightened roar, wings trembling.
“Do not engage them! Stay behind me!” he barked suddenly, his voice sharper, and protective. The Veil hesitated, then slowly pulled back his hood. Alan’s eyes widened in shock. Beneath the shadow of the hood, it wasn’t skin– it was coarse, dark fur. The creatures paused, they regained courage and without thinking they rushed further toward the Veil. The moment they touched him, they wilted, collapsing like withered leaves.
Then silence came. The Veil’s voice was low, almost a whisper, soaked in shame. “Don’t… look at me…” He drew the hood back over his head, hiding his fur, looking down. His posture slumped, the weight of years of curse and regret catching back to him.
After what felt like hours, they reached the base of the Whispering Cliffs. The Veil landed gently, as the dragon followed. “Beyond these cliffs lies the swamp where the witch dwells,” he said, his tone softened with sorrow. “Prepare yourself… the witch does not tolerate lies or half-truths. Hold your courage firm, for I cannot shield you from all that awaits.”
Alan took a deep breath, feeling the Veil’s silent, cautious presence. “I’m ready,” he said.
The Veil’s red eyes glimmered beneath the hood, smirking. “Very well, young Alan,” he said while clapping, “Let us see if courage and sincerity can truly restore what has been lost.”
The dragon stepped forward, stretched his wings as Alan climbed on and hence, they lifted off again, soaring toward the misty swamp beyond the cliffs. Alan clung tightly, hearing the whispering winds grow louder.
As they went further they finally reached… “The Swamp of the Witch,” the Veil said squinting his eyes. The thick fog glided around the tall trees, curling around twisted roots and half-submerged logs. The air was damp, and carried a strange smell which made Alan’s nose twitch. The Veil floated ahead, scanning the swamp carefully. Alan’s dragon hesitated, as his tiny, trembling wings landed on a patch of land. Alan held tightly the dragon scales as he came down with trembling legs.
“Stay close… but not too close,” the Veil called to Alan, “This swamp reacts to fear. Any misstep, and it can drag you under. Either through quicksand or the creatures under the swamp. Am I being understood?”
Alan nodded, stepping cautiously on the land. The swamp seemed alive, green bubbles rising here and there, frogs singing and flies buzzing. The shadows began taking shapes that looked like the villagers he knew… then vanished. He swallowed hard, forcing himself to keep moving.
Suddenly, small, black, eel-like creatures shot from the water, snapping at the dragon. Alan yelped, ducking low. The Veil removed his glove then glared at the creature and said in a threatening manner, “Leave or else…” the creatures, threatening, swam away with great speed.
“This way,” the Veil muttered, guiding them through a narrow path of the swamp. Alan noticed a faint shimmer in the water—mirrors of the swamp that reflected not just their images, but their fears. He caught glimpses of the Veil’s own reflection in the murky water, twisted and monstrous.
“Oh, the Veil,” he murmured, feeling bad for him.
The dragon squeaked, stepping onto a floating patch of moss that wobbled dangerously. Alan shifted carefully, listening to the Veil’s calm but distant directions. “Do not touch the shadows. Do not talk to the reflections of fears that you are seeing on the murky water. Keep your eyes forward. Trust the path.”
A sudden fog followed them, thicker than before, forming shapes, whispering accusations and doubts, trying to confuse them. Alan’s heart pounded heavily, but he clenched his teeth, and went on. Every step required courage and every glance at the Veil reminded him of the unspoken sorrow of a man trapped by his own past. After what felt like hours, they reached a small clearing where the swamp opened into a dark grove. At its center stood a twisted hut, smoke coming out from a crooked chimney. Alan gasped. This was the witch’s home—the final test.
The Veil hovered around, eyes scanning for threats. “Beyond this point… your courage will be tested again, Alan,” he said.
Now, only one challenge remained: facing the witch, and helping the Veil reclaim his humanity.
Alan stepped carefully toward the crooked hut at the center of the swamp. The dragon shifted nervously beneath him, wings twitching.
From somewhere within the hut, a voice cackled—a high, rattling laugh that echoed through the mist. “Hehehe… so, so brave, so curious… and yet, so foolish!” The witch’s laughter echoed around the swamp. Alan shivered, tightening his grip. The laugh was cruel, and carried a strange magic that made the swamp itself seem to shiver in response.
The Veil hovered behind Alan. “Do not speak first,” he warned, voice low. “Let me see her for myself… the curse she wove runs deeper than wrath itself. She will test you.”
Alan’s heart pounded. Another cackle cut through the air:
“Come closer, little boy! Come closer and see the truth of your friend!”
The dragon squeaked, but Alan stayed still. He began taking slow breaths. The Veil’s red eyes glimmered beneath his hood, reflecting both sorrow… and hope. Alan realized: this was not just a test of courage, but a test of sincerity. Another laugh echoed, closer: “Hehe… will you beg, will you cry, or will you fail like the rest?”
Alan gulped, remembering the Veil’s warnings. “I would not falter. I have come this far… and I won’t give up,” Alan whispered to the Veil.
Alan stepped closer to the crooked hut, heart pounding. The witch’s laughter had faded into a low, teasing cackle, echoing.
“So… you’ve brought him back to me?” she hissed, leaning from the doorway, eyes gleaming with mischief. “Do you think words will undo what was done?”
Alan gulped. He glanced at the Veil, hovering just behind him, hood low, red eyes glimmering with sorrow.
The witch’s sharp gaze shifted between them. “He called me ugly… he mocked me!” Her voice rose, trembling with years of built-up bitterness. “Do you understand why I cursed him? Why he walks as a shadow instead of a man?”
Alan nodded, feeling the weight of the journey, the creatures, the swamp, and the Veil’s suffering.
“I… I understand. I’m sorry,” Alan said, carrying the Veil’s sorrow as though it was his own.
The witch’s eyes narrowed. “I am not searching for your apology,” she said as she walked closer to the Veil. “You! Apologize…” she snapped bitterly, pointing a crooked finger. Her cackle rattled the swamp like breaking glass.
The Veil froze, eyes darting everywhere—at Alan, at the witch, at the dragon. “Me…? Apologize?” His voice cracked, pride and embarrassment washing over his face. “I… I don’t even…”
“Yes, you!” the witch shrieked, hopping slightly on her crooked boots. “You called me ugly, mocked me, and now you want to hide behind the boy? Apologize properly, or the curse stays!”
Alan tried to hide a smile. The sight of the high and mighty, terrifying Veil flustered and mumbling was almost too funny. The dragon let out a small, squeaky roar, adding to the absurdity.
“N-Never!” the Veil shouted, causing the witch to stomp her foot angrily. Alan and the dragon stopped laughing. “And why is that so?” she asked, eyebrow twitching.
“I’m handsome, rich, and have all that any soul could desire—women, gold, luxury. You’re but a grumpy, crooked old hag! I’ll ne’er apologize. I am above thee, and the world itself bends beneath my finger,” the Veil said, fantasizing about his non-existent belongings… with exaggerated pride. “Are you hearing what he said, oh you, the boy with the satchel?” the witch asked, glaring at Alan.
“Y-Yes,” Alan stammered.
“That’s almost the exact same thing he said which caused me to curse him,” the witch replied, her voice trembling as tears rolled down her cheeks. “I told him my feelings… that I liked him… but instead of simply accepting it or apologizing, he insulted me and belittled me,” the witch said, tears rolling down her cheeks.
Alan looked at the Veil, disappointment heavy in his gaze. “Haven’t you learnt from your mistake?”
The witch’s cries grew, like an adolescent heartbroken for the first time. The Veil flinched, stepping back, then slowly came closer, memories of his cruel words flashing before his eyes. Remorse knotted his chest.
“O you, the witch of Elsmore’s darkest forest,” he said softly, causing her to lift her head toward him.
“I… I am sorry,” the Veil whispered, tears rolling down his face. The witch’s eyes widened, caught off guard by his sincerity.
“Ethan… are you apologizing to me?” the witch asked, and Alan blinked in surprise, he hadn’t known the Veil’s real name. “Ethan?” Alan wondered.
“Yes… I am. Are you happy now?” Ethan said, his cheeks tinged with pink. He felt embarrassed; this was the first apology in his thousand-year life. The witch wiped her tears and smiled. Her features softened, growing younger, and delicate wings sprouted from her back. She transformed into… the forgotten pixie queen, Alisa.
“Thank you, Ethan,” Alisa said, waving her previously crooked stick, wand gracefully.
“Abracadabra… The curse is lifted; let the darkness become light,” she chanted as she began to float. She tapped her wand lightly on Ethan’s nose, and the swamp around them shimmered and transformed. Colors spinned and turned through the air, trees blossomed, and the mist cleared.
Ethan began to float, spinning gently as pixies swirled around him. He removed his hood—his black hair gleamed, the fur disappeared, and his red eyes turned golden and human. A white robe settled elegantly over his shoulders as his hood became a pourpoint.
Alan gasped. “You… you are Ethan, the Duke of Whitestone, who came to visit Elsmore for hunting… then disappeared?”
“Cursed, rather,” Ethan corrected elegantly, running his fingers through his hair.
“So, the witch of Elsmore is… the missing pixie queen of Fairyland, Alisa,” Alan realized.
Alisa chuckled, and said gently “I wanted to test you. To see just how far a human’s pride could go… well for the least, a half-human, half-elf,” she said, and Alan noticed his pointed ears.
“And by doing so… you made the pixies cry over you for centuries,” Ethan murmured, “What if I had never apologized?”
“I would have eventually returned,” Alisa replied softly.
“Of course,” Ethan glared, his tone sarcastic.
The once-dark swamp became a sparkling spring, and the whispering cliffs turned into white mountains. Elsmore had transformed, and so had Ethan.
“Oh yes, what’s your name, little dragon?” asked Alan patting the dragon. The little dragon roared as his tail wagged, Ethan nodded in comprehension and said, “His name is Blaze.”
“Oh,” Alan gasped. Ethan, Alisa, Blaze and Alan made their way back to the village as sunset arrived.
When they entered Elsmore, the villagers were in a craze. People rushed toward them, worry showing on every face. “Alan! Where have you been? We’ve been so worried!” one villager cried. Mothers clutched their children; friends looked pale with concern. Alan’s dragon let out a soft squeak as he landed gently on the village square, the little creature now calm and playful.
Alan smiled, brushing dirt from his clothes. “I’m fine… thanks to them,” he said, glancing at Ethan and Alisa. The villagers froze, their eyes widening as they took in Ethan—the Veil now back to his original form, tall, noble—and Alisa, radiant as the forgotten pixie queen, the sunset rays passing through her wings.
Grandmothers and grandfathers of the village whispered to each other, leaning on walking sticks and canes. “Could it… could it really be them?” one elderly woman murmured.
“The stories… our grandparents told us,” an old man said, voice trembling, “about the Duke of Whitestone and the Pixie Queen of Fairyland… could it truly be them, returned at last?”
Children ran to touch Ethan’s robe and marvel at Alisa’s wings, while the adults stepped back in confusion.
Alan’s grandmother, Gladys, stepped forward, eyes wide but warm. “So the tales of old were true,” she said, amazed.
Alisa smiled gently. “We have returned to ensure Elsmore remembers the magic, the courage, and the kindness that binds all of us," she said calmly.
Ethan stepped forward, looking at the crowd. “There is something you should all know,” he said calmly, “I… I am the Veil.”
Murmurs and gasps flew across the crowd as everyone processed his words. Ethan raised a hand toward Alan. “And it is because of this brave boy that I have been given a second chance to be myself again, and to return to the world I lost.”
The villagers stared in shock for a moment, then slowly, wonder and joy replaced disbelief. Cheers and laughter echoed through the streets. The sun cast a golden glow over Elsmore. For the first time in centuries, the village felt more alive. Alan looked at his friends and family. Change had come—not just to Ethan and Alisa—but to Elsmore itself. And with it, a new chapter of hope and magic began.
Years later…
“The stories of old were never meant to be forgotten,” said Alan.
“Alan, come, let’s take a picture!” a girl called to him, Alan, now an adolescent. “Coming,” Alan replied as the wind swept across the meadows, sending dandelions swirling around him.
“Grandma, I’m preserving the stories,” Alan said with a smile, gazing at the picture of his late grandmother in his hand.
He put the picture in his pocket and began running through the meadows and reached the picture area where everyone was gathered—Ethan and Alisa, unchanged by time; the dragon, now larger; and Elsmore, a grander scene than before.
“Say ‘stone cakes!’” called the flying gargoyle as it hovered near the camera obscura.
“Stone cakes!” They all chorused, and the picture was taken.
…and so, the tales of Elsmore lived on.
