Ravi, the Rich Boy 💔

In the heart of Jaipur, where palaces shone under the sun and the air smelled of jalebi and pani puri, lived Ravi, a boy who had everything. His father owned half the city’s marble trade, and their home was full of tall pillars, soft silk curtains, and servants who rushed when he called.
It was his tenth birthday.
The house was buzzling, filled with laughter, balloons, and ribbons.
Gifts covered the marble table, shining under the lights like tiny treasures.
“Hey, look!” Ravi called to his friends, waving a new fountain pen.
“Papa brought this from Dubai! It writes smoother than yours.”
A few boys laughed, but some looked away feeling second-hand embarrassment.
Ravi didn’t notice — perhaps he didn’t want to.
He liked being the center of attention, the one with shiny things and louder words— the center of attention.
From upstairs, Ravi could hear his mother’s soft, wheezing cough, reminding him of her illness.
She hadn’t come down to the party; she was too weak.
Ravi barely glanced toward her room— excitement and pride filled his heart more than worry, worry of his own mother’s health.
During the party, Ravi spotted two classmates, twins from a wealthy family who preferred simple things, sitting quietly at the edge of the crowd.
“This is what you give someone for their birthday?” Ravi snickered, pointing at their modest gift-wrapped box.
“You could’ve asked your parents for something better.”
The twins looked away, cheeks flushed.
One whispered, “We like it this way.”
Ravi shrugged.
“Suit yourselves.
Guess not everyone knows what real taste is, but I humbly accept your gift.”
“‘Humbly’ he says,” someone in the crowd scoffed.
Ravi, ignoring the scoff, went on pinpointing people’s gifts.
The next day at Maharaja Academy, the children of Jaipur’s elite studied under white domes and banyan trees.
Ravi strutted through the courtyard, brandishing his new pen and gifts, eager to show off.
“Hey, look!” he called to his friends again.
“Papa brought this from Tokyo! It’s way better than yours!”
Laughter erupted, but not all laughed.
During recess, he teased the twins once more, but this around it was about their plain lunch boxes.
The teasing was constant, and offensive.
Whispers soon reached the teachers.
The bullying wasn’t harsh, but it was enough to draw attention.
That afternoon, a Lamborghini black car rolled up outside the academy.
All eyes turned.
It was Mr. Sharma — Ravi’s father.
The man who rarely came.
His face was unreadable, his demeanor intimidating.
Ravi came out to see his dad.
“Good morning, Papa!” said Ravi, smiling.
“Ravi,” he said quietly, “we need to talk.”
Ravi froze.
Inside the principal’s office, he rocked his chair playing with his fingers while humming to himself.
His father stood near the window, posture perfect with a straight face.
“Your teachers say you mock others,” his father finally said, “That you measure worth by what people wear and own.”
Ravi gulped.
“I was just… joking.”
“Jokes don’t leave wounds, Ravi,” Mr. Sharma said quietly, turning to glance at his hands.
“I built everything we have with these hands, but if wealth makes you forget respect… then maybe I built it wrong.”
Ravi looked up, desperate for even a small sign of forgiveness.
But his father remained silent, never smiling, his eyes piercing straight into Ravi’s soul.
“He’ll apologize,” Mr. Sharma told the principal.
“Yes, sir,” Ravi murmured, voice breaking.
The next morning, Ravi walked into class without his usual grin.
His friends whispered, expecting jokes or stories about new gifts, but he said nothing.
At lunch, he sat alone under the banyan tree, staring at the steel lunchbox his mother had packed.
Her note on the lid — “Eat well, my sunshine” — comforted him.
His friends whispered nearby.
“Ravi’s quiet today.”
“Maybe his dad scolded him.”
“Good. Someone needed to.”
Ravi heard them, their words stinging.
“I was just joking, did it really hurt them that much?” Ravi wondered to himself.
When he returned home, the house felt quiet and clean.
The laughter that used to be filled died down.
Servants moved quietly, afraid to disturb the silence his father’s presence left behind.
He climbed the marble staircase slowly.
At his mother’s door, he paused hearing her cough.
“Maa?” he called softly.
“Yes, my dear,” she answered, her voice faint and tired.
He hesitated, fingers on the doorknob, then stepped back.
For the first time, he didn’t want to brag.
He just stood there, listening to her weak breathing.
“Why are you always sick? Why is Dad never at home?” Ravi asked, tears welling up.
“My dear son… come in,” she said, coughing.
She lay in bed, strands of her hair white, as she adjusted her shawl.
She patted the bed, motioning him to sit.
Ravi obeyed, folding his hands, tears still trickling down.
His mother wiped his tears gently.
“Ravi, my child, why must you always complain?”
“You have food, clothes, a roof, and love, my dear,” she whispered. “There are children who sleep hungry, who dream of what you have.”
“But Maa,” Ravi muttered, “what’s the point of all this gold and marble if you’re always sick? If Papa never comes home?”
Her eyes softened.
“Your father works hard so you may never feel the hunger he once knew.
And I… I am only sick in body, not in heart.
But, Ravi, my worry is not my illness.
It’s you.”
“Me?” he asked, blinking.
“You chase wealth as if it will fill you,” she whispered, gazing at the ceiling.
“Gold is only heavy, my son.
When you hold too much of it, you start dropping the things that matter.”
Ravi looked down, silent.
He didn’t fully understand, but her frail tone made him.
“Promise me one thing, Ravi,” she said gently.
“Cherish people, not possessions.
Because when people go… no amount of gold can call them back.”
He nodded weakly, eyes glassy.
“I promise, Maa.”
She smiled faintly.
“That’s my boy.”
The clock ticked quietly.
Outside, the wind blew against the window, creating a rattling sound.
Ravi sat there long after she drifted to sleep, holding her hand.
For the first time, gold didn’t look beautiful.
It looked… cold.
The next morning, Ravi sat at the breakfast table, fingers drumming impatiently.
“I am hungry! I want food!” Ravi complained.
“After everything, I told him yesterday, he still hasn’t mend his ways,” the mother thought to herself.
“Ravi, enough with this show every single day!” his mother snapped, her voice sharp despite her weakness.
“You cannot treat people like this. Stop bragging, stop throwing things around, and help in the house for once!”
Ravi scowled. “Maa, I—”
“No, Ravi! You do not get to interrupt me! Just listen for once!” she cried, clutching the edges of her chair.
Why was she so angry today? She rarely reacted like this.
Her doctor had told her she wouldn’t live long, and she didn’t want to leave her son with a spoiled, arrogant attitude.
For days she had repeated the same warnings, and for days Ravi had ignored them.
He strutted through the halls, tossing toys, stomping across marble floors, and laughing as his friends gawked at his wealth.
One afternoon, Ravi was AGAIN bragging about a new fountain pen when his mother’s cough sounded harsher.
He looked up, eyes widening.
“Maa?” he called as she clutched her chest, gasping, eyes wide with sudden fear.
“Call… the doctor!” she wheezed.
The maid rushed in, but it was too late.
Ravi’s mother had collapsed.
Panic rose as the maids tried to carefully carry her.
The car sped toward the hospital, Ravi holding her hand, tears streaming, heart pounding.
When they arrived, his father stormed in, face pale but angry.
“What happened?!” he barked.
The maid stammered, “S-sir… Ravi… he—he wasn’t listening. He was—”
Ravi’s father turned toward him. His mother, lying weakly on the hospital bed, coughed, her hand trembling as she reached for Ravi.
“Ravi… arrange my room. The maid won’t be here forever.
And stop throwing things around,” she whispered, voice frail but firm. “You must listen…”
Ravi froze, guilt stabbing him.“I… I didn’t… I…”
Before he could finish, his mother’s hand dropped, her body trembling violently.The doctors rushed in. Ravi’s father grabbed him by the shoulder. “Do you understand what happens when you ignore those who care for you, Ravi? When you let your arrogance run wild? Do you?!” he said in a harsh manner.
Ravi gulped and remained speechless.
Tears ran down his face as he looked at the stretcher. The weight of every word, every boastful laugh, every ignored warning coming down on him.
After the doctors took her in, Ravi ran from the hospital and out into the hospital’s parking lot, curling into himself on the curb.He had been too blind, too selfish, too full of pride.
The next day at Maharaja Academy, Ravi returned, but he was not the same. He sat alone under the banyan tree, staring at the ground, his new pen untouched in his bag.
“Ravi?” came a familiar voice.
Oswald, one of his closest friends and also wealthy, walked up, eyebrows raised.“What happened to you, my fellow gentleman? You—”
Ravi looked up, eyes red and swollen. “I… I can’t… I can’t talk about it,” he whispered.
Oswald frowned, unsure. “You… you look different. You got conjunctivitis?”
“No, I… just want to change.” Ravi muttered, voice barely audible.
“Everything… I’ve been doing… it was wrong.”
Oswald blinked.“Change? You mean… stop bragging?”
Ravi shook his head. “More than that… I need to be better… for Maa.”
Oswald’s mouth opened, then closed. He didn’t know what to say. For the first time, Ravi wasn’t the boy with everything. He was just a boy who had realized how fragile the world could be, how easily pride could hurt.
Ravi visited his mother every morning, lingering by her bedside, holding her frail hand, whispering stories from school, or the smallest things that made her smile.
He had stopped bragging, stopped teasing, stopped thinking about wealth. All that mattered was her.
Days passed slowly. Sometimes, her coughs were sharper, her breaths shallower.
“Maa… stay with me, please,” he whispered one evening, voice shaking. “Don’t leave me alone.”
His mother, Kiara, smiled weakly, her eyelids fluttering. “My son… you’ve grown… faster than I expected,” she said softly. “I’m proud… of you, Ravi.”
“No! Don’t… don’t say that!” he cried. “I haven’t done anything yet! I need you!”
The nurse came in, shaking her head gently. “Ravi… she’s very tired today… we must let her rest.”
After the nurse injected her with medication, he came back and sat beside his mother. He whispered promises to be kind, to listen, to change his ways forever.
Ravi slept there. During the evening, Ravi still clutched the hospital bed, his small hands trembling and cold against his mother’s. His eyes opened at the sound of silence, only to turn and find her chest still, her hand slipping from his, and the steady beeping of the cardiac monitor.
“Maa? Maa! No!” he screamed, clutching her, rocking back and forth. He immediately pressed the nurse call button.
“Mom… please… don’t go,” he whispered, voice breaking. “I’ll be good, I promise!”
Tears streamed down his cheeks, and he pressed his face into her shoulder. “I… I didn’t know, Maa… I was so… so stupid.”
A nurse entered quietly, placing a comforting hand on Ravi’s shoulder, but he barely noticed. He just held her, hoping she could hear him, hoping she could stay. But the beeping of the machines slowed, then stopped.
“No… no…” he whispered, voice cracking, as he realized she was gone. The room felt impossibly silent, the air heavy and empty.
The day was gray, clouds hanging low over Jaipur, the air thick and silent. Ravi walked behind his mother’s body, wrapped simply in white cloth, her small form still and fragile.
His father, Sharma, followed close, dark blue eyes depressed, black hair scattered across his pale forehead.
The man who had always been calm, reserved, rarely showing emotion, now trembled as if the loss had shattered something deep within. His lips were pale, his fists clenched at his sides.
As they reached the edge of the grave, Sharma’s knees buckled slightly. He bent forward, voice breaking as he whispered, “Kiara… my love… why… why now?”
His grief poured out, bitter and raw. Tears streamed down his face, falling onto the white cloth that wrapped Kiara.
He had never cried like this, never allowed himself such vulnerability, yet now the loss brought out this form of himself.
Ravi ran to him, tears streaking his own cheeks. “Papa… she’s… she’s gone, mommy is… gone,” he sobbed.
“You little brat!” Sharma barked, pacing, fists clenched, voice shaking. “How could you be so careless?! I… I gave you one job! Just one… be obedient… be good to her… and now she’s gone!”
Sharma fell onto the ground, sobbing in a way Ravi had never imagined his father was capable of. “Just one… just one…” he whispered brokenly. “Be nice… to your mother… and now… now…”
Kiara’s brother, Adithya, approached calmly, placing a hand on Sharma’s trembling back. “It’s okay, my brother. Your grief will not bring her peace if it consumes you,” he said gently. Sharma’s sobs slowed, his eyes remained hollow and distant, staring as they laid Kiara to rest.
For days afterward, Ravi could hardly eat or sleep. He wandered through the hollow house. The marble floors gleamed, the silk curtains swayed, but there was no warmth, no laughter. Only the echo of loss.
At school, the change in him was immediate.
No longer did he boast or bully; he walked quietly, head bowed, small steel lunchbox in hand. Oswald, his former best friend, looked at him curiously.
“Ravi… what has happened to you again? First we meet at the banyan tree, now under the mango tree,” he asked one day under the mango tree.
Ravi’s lips trembled. “I… I lost someone I loved… and I realized money can’t bring them back,” he whispered, clutching the crumbled note his mother had tucked into his lunchbox: “Eat well, my sunshine.”
Oswald nodded, finally understanding. “Oh, that… that actually makes sense,” he said softly.
Three months later, Ravi approached his father in the grand study.
“Papa,” he said quietly, “I am sorry for what I have caused. I want to go to a normal school. I want to understand life… really live… not just have gold around me.”
Sharma looked at him, the man who had rarely smiled, studied his son, then gently ruffled his black hair. “I forgive you, Ravi. You have learned the lesson that money cannot buy everything… and I am proud of you.”
At the new school, Ravi felt like an oddball at first. No golden pens, no silk uniforms, no servants attending him.
But he discovered the richness of kindness, friendship, and laughter. He helped classmates, shared stories, and learned that the world held treasures far beyond marble and money.
He met Ayaan, who loved books, and Sara, whose passion was dancing. Ravi found joy in giving and caring rather than receiving, understanding that wealth was nothing if hearts were empty.
At home, Ravi and Sharma slowly rebuilt their relationship. They cooked meals together, shared memories of Kiara, and sometimes went golfing.
Years later, Ravi turned into a man who worked hard, loved his family, and led a humble life.
When he got married and had kids, he passed on what grief had taught him: that wealth is nothing without kindness, and losing someone shows you just how much people matter.
Most evenings, you’d find Ravi and Sharma sitting together. Sharma’s dark blue eyes had softened over the years. The two of them shared lessons about sorrow, humility, and love—not with fancy things, but with hearts that had been reshaped by everything they’d been through.
Ravi, who once used to be the spoiled kid from Jaipur, finally understood what real wealth meant. It’s not something you can hold or show off. It’s something you carry inside you. Never in hands, only in hearts.

 “You never know the value of a moment until it becomes a memory.” -Theodor Seuss Geisel
