Samiya, the Astronaut 🌌

In the city of Doha, where skyscrapers sliced through the desert heat, a classroom buzzed with energy and ambition.
Amidst the chatter and the clatter of pens of the students, one girl stood out, Samiya, a Qatari student whose imagination stretched beyond the stars.
“I want to travel past the meteor belt,” said Samiya, her brown eyes shining with enthusiasm.
Mrs. Abinaya put down her marker and turned toward the young girl who spoke in the middle of class.
“Samiya, that is not possible,” she said calmly.
“Not possible yet,” Samiya replied quietly, smiling to herself.
The class burst into laughter.
“Oh really?” said a boy from the back. “And what will you use? A rocket made of cardboard?”
“Maybe,” said Samiya, unbothered, flipping open her notebook where pages were filled with complex sketches of spacecraft, asteroid maps, and equations.
Mrs. Abinaya smiled in amusement. “Alright, Dr. Samiya Einstein. If you believe so strongly, then prove me wrong someday.”
That sentence, ‘Prove me wrong someday,’ stayed with Samiya for years as a challenge…and a goal.
After class, she stayed behind, packing her books and arranging the classroom. The room was quiet except for the faint whirring of the ceiling fan.
“Samiya,” said Mrs. Abinaya, walking over. “You’re a bright student, but sometimes, you dream too far. The meteor belt is dangerous; no human has ever gone beyond it.”
“That’s exactly why I want to,” Samiya replied. “Because no one else has.”
The teacher, amazed, then said gently, “Curiosity is beautiful, but ambition needs patience.”
Samiya nodded. “Then I’ll be patient until I reach the stars.”
That evening, Samiya sat by her small balcony, looking up at the sky. The stars blinked faintly as she began singing ‘Little Little Twinkle Star.’
She whispered, “One day… I’ll see what lies beyond you.”
Her younger brother, Arif, peeked out from behind the curtain. “Are you talking to stars again?”
“Maybe yes, maybe no, maybe we’ll never know,” she said with a smirk.
He laughed and said, “If you are, then tell them I said hi.”
Samiya chuckled softly. “Okay, Captain Arif.”
Days turned into months, and months turned into years.
Samiya graduated from high school and was still the same as always: buried in research and sci-fi comics.
Books about astrophysics, NASA archives, and propulsion mechanics.
She even joined an online group of space enthusiasts, where she met people from different parts of the world.
One evening, while browsing an old space forum, she stumbled upon something surprising: a journal entry from 2078 by a forgotten astronaut named Dr. Elara Voss.
It read:
“They said the meteor belt was a wall, but walls only exist for those who stop climbing.”
“Elara Voss… from a year we haven’t reached yet,” she whispered, curiosity blazing.
She searched everywhere but found no record of the astronaut. No archives, no pictures. Only a series of handwritten pages that seemed to have been uploaded by an anonymous user.
Each page contained designs, sketches, and handwritten calculations that seemed ages ahead of current technology.
“Separation of water to obtain oxygen… This… this can’t be real,” Samiya murmured.
But something inside her said it was.
The next afternoon, after a long day of university lectures, Samiya hurried across the campus courtyard.
She clutched a worn-out folder tightly against her chest.
She burst into the engineering lab, where her friend Rahma was soldering wires onto a circuit board.
“Rahma, look!” Samiya exclaimed, startling Rahma.
Samiya spread the papers on the table.
“These are the notes of Elara Voss. An astronaut who claimed to have designed a propulsion system strong enough to pass the meteor belt.”
Rahma blinked, adjusting her glasses as she leaned closer.
The papers were blue and wrinkled, filled with complex diagrams, numbers, and scribbled annotations in faded ink.
“This looks… old,” Rahma murmured, tracing a line with her finger. “Where did you find this?”
“On a private archive forum,” Samiya replied, her voice barely containing her excitement.
“Most people say it’s a joke, but… Look at these formulas. They’re decades ahead of their time. I think it’s real.”
Rahma frowned slightly. “Even if it’s not,” she said smiling, “perhaps it can teach you something.”
Samiya then flipped the pages of the book and saw a page having a simple star drawn beside the words ‘For those who dare to dream.’
She smiled faintly. “Yes, to never give up on a dream.”
Rahma put her tools aside and turned to face Samiya fully.
“Samiya,” she began, “I admire your passion, but you’ve been so focused on the cosmos lately. Everyday cosmos, everyday cosmos. Do you not think of other things that could be built? Things that might actually reach people here on Earth.”
Samiya frowned a bit. “Rahma, I did not expect you also to be judgmental on my dream, but anyways,” she sighed, then chuckled.
“A dream is a thing that can be done,” she said, her tone filled with determination. “Elara believed it. I believe it too.”
Rahma sighed, then smiled. “Then promise me one thing, that you’ll dream wisely.”
“I will,” Samiya said with a soft smile. “But even wise dreams need a little madness.”
Rahma nudged Samiya with a frown and said, “You little Einstein head!”
Then they both looked at each other.
The two friends burst into laughter, their voices echoing through the quiet lab.
Outside, thunder cracked across the city as the rain lashed the windowpanes.
Samiya stayed awake, sketching new designs by the light of her desk lamp in her room.
“If her calculations were right,” she muttered, “then we just need to adjust the energy flow to stabilize thrust before the debris field…”
“I hope that this works,” said Samiya, looking at her blueprint sketches.
Weeks later, the University of Cosmos Engineering announced its annual Innovation Science Fair.
Samiya decided to present her invention called Plasma Drive X, a design meant to make spacecrafts travel faster and farther by using charged particles as thrust.
Night after night, she worked, joining the wires and adjusting models on her laptop.
She built a miniature spacecraft inspired by Elara’s notes.
Her mother often peeked in and sighed. “You should rest, Samiya.”
“I’ll rest later on, Mom,” she’d reply, eyes glued to the screen.
The day of the competition arrived.
The auditorium was filled with students.
Judges in suits walked around, evaluating each project.
Samiya stood behind her table, her small spacecraft model gleaming under the lights.
However, during her live demonstration, the model encountered an error and exploded with a loud pop.
The crowd gasped, some students giggled, and the judges whispered among themselves.
“You made people think,” said her mother, “and that’s where discovery begins.”
Samiya began crying as her mother hugged her. “Then maybe I’m already halfway there.”
That night, Samiya looked out at the stars again.
“Elara Voss,” she whispered, “I wonder where you are.”
And in the silence of the night, a faint meteor streaked across the sky, determining her not to lose hope.
For months afterward, Samiya dedicated herself to improvement.
She researched, studied, and rebuilt.
By graduation, her new model, Plasma Drive X-2, was stable and powerful.
On the day of her graduation, Mrs. Abinaya attended the ceremony.
“You’ve done it, Samiya,” she said proudly.
“Not yet,” Samiya smiled. “This is just the beginning.”
After university, she received a message from an aerospace organization that had followed her progress online.
“Ms. Rahim,” the voice on the phone said, “we’ve reviewed your research on plasma propulsion. We’d like to invite you to join our astronaut training program.”
Samiya froze. “Me? Are you serious?”
“Completely. You’ve built what others only dream of.”
Her heart raced. The dream that began in her classroom years ago was becoming real.
…Two Years Later…
The training was tough: months of simulations, technical tests, and physical endurance.
But to reach her goal, she viewed it all as a tiny grain of sand in the desert, a phase of life.
When the results came, her name stood first on the list. She was officially an astronaut.
Mrs. Abinaya wrote to her, “You are living proof that dreams powered by knowledge can reach beyond limits.”
Samiya smiled as she read the letter, whispering, “Thank you, ma’am.”
…The Day of the Launch…
Her spaceship, the Celestia, shone under the bright lights.
It had the same engine design she once built in her dorm room.
“Mission goal,” said the commander, “reach the moon’s orbit, test the plasma engine, and return safely.”
Samiya nodded. Deep down, she wanted to go even farther.
The countdown began. “Ten… nine… eight…”
She took a deep breath. “I got this,” she whispered.
“Three… two… one… ignition!”
The engines roared, and Earth slowly shrank below her.
Everything went well until a storm of space rocks hit near the moon.
The plasma engine flickered, and the ship spun out of control.
“Control, we’ve lost our path!” she shouted.
“Stabilize, Samiya! Do you hear me?” crackled the radio.
“Copy that,” she said, her hands shaking. “Switching to manual control!”
She focused hard, moving the ship through the flying debris.
Time passed fast, and then everything became quiet.
The stars outside looked brighter than ever.
Her screen blinked.
Coordinates: Unknown Sector, Beyond Meteor Belt.
She gasped. “I did it.”
Back on Earth, the control room filled with cheers.
“She’s past the meteor belt! She made it!”
Mrs. Abinaya smiled softly. “She aimed for the moon and reached the stars.”
In zero gravity, Samiya looked out the window.
Her tears floated beside her.
“I wanted to pass the meteor belt,” she whispered, “but maybe fate wanted me to reach the stars.”
She pressed the radio button. “Mission complete. Returning to base.”
As The Celestia turned home, space sparkled around her.
“This is just the beginning,” she whispered. “The universe is waiting.”
That night, Samiya sat on her balcony, looking at the stars.
The meteor belt stayed in her mind, reminding her of all she had overcome.
She opened her notebook and wrote:
When you lose sight, look to the stars.
Years later…
The control room was full of energy.
Samiya, now a captain, adjusted her visor.
Her new ship, Aurora’s Edge, shone under the lights, ready for takeoff.
“Mission status?” she asked.
“All systems green, Captain,” said Liora, her young co-pilot, smiling with excitement.
Samiya smiled back.
She remembered when her first small experiment had exploded in front of the judges.
“I went from nobody to somebody,” she whispered.
The countdown began again.
“Ten… nine… eight…”
“Three… two… one… ignition!”
Aurora’s Edge shot upward, light bursting from its engines.
Stars flashed past like silver lines.
Samiya glanced at the meteor belt that once scared her.
“Not today,” she thought. “This time, I’ll glide through.”
Ahead, a colorful nebula glowed in violet and gold.
The view filled her heart with wonder, just like the nights she spent dreaming at her desk.
“Captain, message incoming,” said Liora.
Samiya pressed a button.
Mrs. Abinaya’s gentle yet aged voice came through.
“Congratulations, Samiya. You’ve come far and inspired every young dreamer who looks up at the stars.”
Samiya smiled. “Thank you, ma’am. We’ll keep reaching higher.”
As the ship passed smoothly through the meteor belt, Samiya whispered, “If you ever get lost, look at the stars.”
And as the endless universe stretched before her, she knew—she had finally found her place among the stars… and beyond.

“Shoot for the moon. Even if you miss, you’ll land among the stars.” -Norman Vincent Peale
