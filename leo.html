Leo, the Young Writer ✍🏻

In the heart of France, lived young Leo de Montreux, son of Duke Adrien.
Their home was full of marble, silk, and the busy footsteps of busy servants.
Outside, the town bustled with merchants, children, and dogs, a world far different from Leo’s luxurious life.
Leo, seven, sat on a velvet cushion in the grand study, writing stories in secret— tales of streets, laughter, and struggles beyond the estate, beyond what he even knew.
“Leonardo!” his father’s voice echoed.
“What is this nonsense? You are a duke’s son! Practice the piano! Music, discipline, duty!” he barked, his face reddening.
He wore his usual white peruke and a justaucorps of royal cloth.
Leo lowered his eyes. “Yes, Papa,” he whispered, obeying. Still, the stories in his mind didn’t go.
One morning, he asked, “Papa… may I go with Madame Elise to the market? Just to see the streets?”
Duke Adrien frowned. “A duke’s son does not wander the streets. Stick to your lessons and social etiquette.”
“But Papa, I just want to see!” Leo pleaded.
After a pause, the Duke waved his hand dismissively, sighing as if tired. “Vraiment, Leonardo… sors de ma vue.” Leave my sight. “Go with the maid, but stay out of trouble, Leo.”
Excited, Leo followed Madame Elise down the cobbled streets. The smell of fresh bread and roasting chestnuts filled the air, mixed with children’s laughter and bakers calling to customers.
“Monsieur Leonardo, I shall be going to the bakery. Please remain here and be an obedient boy. Do you understand?” said Madame Elise.
Leonardo nodded, and Madame went off to buy the bread.
At a small stall, he saw a poor boy, looking at a loaf of bread in a hungry manner. Leo reached into his pocket and handed him a few livres. Livres was the currency used in medieval France Era.
The boy’s eyes widened in surprise. Leo smiled softly.
“Thank you so much!” the boy said, shaking Leonardo’s hand.
“My pleasure, monsieur,” Leo replied, startled by the boy’s excitement.
“Oh, he seems to be someone from the Second Estate,” the boy thought to himself.
“May I know your name, monsieur?” asked Leonardo curiously.
The boy smiled, showing his broken front teeth. “My name’s William, a farmer’s child,” he said.
“My name is Leonardo de Montreux,” Leo replied, unintentionally drawing the crowd’s attention. Murmurs began to spread:
“A Montreux in these streets?”
“Duke Adrien’s child!”
“Oh my! What is a noble’s child doing here?”
As Madame Elise took the bread bag, she turned to see a crowd forming around Leonardo—and a peasant boy. Without hesitation, she hurried over, took Leonardo’s hand, and led him away.
“Bye!” the boy called, waving. “I hope we meet again!”
“I hope so! Bye, William!” Leonardo answered, waving back.
Back home, the grand halls seemed quieter than before.
Duke Adrien returned to his papers, unaware of the small act that had already begun shaping his son into someone different—someone whose words would one day weigh heavier than a sword, and whose grace would outshine wealth itself.
The Montreux estate was always silent in the early mornings, except for the footsteps of servants and the clink of vases in the dining hall. Leo sat at the table, a stack of books before him. He at once opened the books and began reading.
His tutor, Monsieur Duval, peered over his shoulder, frowning at the young boy’s distracted face.
“Leonardo, you are to memorize Racine’s Andromaque by tonight, not doodle in the margins!” the tutor scolded, tapping his cane.
“Yes… yes, Monsieur,” Leo murmured, pushing the small sketches aside.
The morning lessons followed their usual order: French literature, Latin, arithmetic, social etiquettes and history. Monsieur Duval’s sharp voice often reminded him, “A Montreux must know all—history, honor, and virtue—lest the family name suffer.”
But Leo focused on only one vision: a world where justice reached beyond the walls of nobility.
That afternoon, the piano awaited him.
Leo followed the piano score à la lettre.
Each note he played reminded him of his father’s expectations—perfection, discipline, nobility—all of which suffocated him.
“Leo,” his mother’s voice echoed down the hallway, “you must practice diligently! A Montreux must impress the people with beauty and skill.”
In the evenings, Leo often sat at his small desk by the window, writing by candlelight—quiet stories that only he would read.
Months passed. Lessons, piano, and etiquette filled his days, yet Leo’s thoughts always drifted to the world beyond the Montreux estate—to the streets.
He hoped to meet William again, to tell him that his view of the world had changed.
Sadly, his father no longer allowed him to visit the cobbled streets of Marseille.
By eighteen, Leonardo had grown into a handsome young man. His attendants combed his blond hair as he looked into the mirror. He dressed with elegance.
Everything about him reflected the grace of his noble birth, yet beneath that refinement laid a humbler soul.
One evening, the Princess of the Lysandre Kingdom, Rose, approached him with a smile.
Her pink hair and violet eyes made her radiant.
Leonardo stepped forward and kissed her hand—a gesture of respect and elegance in France.
“My greetings, Mademoiselle,” he said softly.
She chuckled.
“Shall we get going, Monsieur?”
“Why, indeed. Let’s go,” he replied, aware of his father’s sharp gaze from behind, warning him to behave properly before his cavalier—for his first ball, and before a princess of higher rank.
The carriage arrived. They stepped inside and departed for the ball held at the Château de Les Blues.
“Could you tell me about life in Marseille?” she asked.
“It is very… endearing,” he said carefully, guarding the family’s honor.
When they arrived at the château, Leonardo stepped out first and helped Mlle. Rose down from the carriage.
“Shall we?” he asked, his emerald eyes shining in the moonlight.
“Y-Yes, please,” she answered, slightly flustered.
The carriage left to fetch his parents as the ball commenced. Leonardo mingled with nobles, his tone eloquent and composed.
“Monsieur Leonardo,” Rose said, “I’ve heard you play the piano beautifully. Will you honor us tonight?”
“My pleasure, Your Grace,” Leonardo replied, bowing slightly, his calm and refined voice drawing attention without ever demanding it.
From the corner, his mother beamed. “Yes! My son will perform and astonish them all!” she whispered to a neighbor, eyes sparkling with pride.
Across the room, his father observed in silence, sharp blue eyes noting every gesture—Leonardo’s composure, his elegance, and the quiet dignity that set him apart.
Leonardo seated himself at the grand piano.
Fingers poised over the ivory keys, he began to play.
The first notes flowed with precision and beauty.
The crowd fell silent, all eyes fixed on the young Montreux heir.
A young lord whispered, “Where did he learn to master the instrument with such precision?”
“Monsieur Duval,” another noble replied.
“You mean the grand duke’s son’s piano master?!” the first exclaimed, his astonishment increasing the pride of Leonardo’s parents.
Princess Rose took up a violin and joined him, their melodies mixing into a graceful symphony. When the final note lingered, the hall erupted in applause. Leonardo and Rose rose and bowed with grace.
The Princess of Lysandre tilted her head, intrigued.
“Leonardo, what inspires your music—the gardens, the courts… or something more?”
“All of it, Your Grace,” he replied softly. “The world around me—the joys, the sorrows, even the unseen hardships—all find their way into my music.”
Murmurs rippled through the room. His mother clutched her fan. “He is truly extraordinary! The Montreux legacy shines brighter than ever!” she whispered proudly.
A nobleman, intrigued, asked next. “Leonardo, do you intend to pursue public life, perhaps serve the court?”
“I intend to observe, to learn, and perhaps serve where I might do the most good,” he replied.
That night, Leonardo silently reaffirmed his promise: one day, he would wield words and ideas for a world that honored justice.
The next day, the estate went on with the same routines. Leonardo sat at the long table, quill in hand, while Monsieur Duval walked nearby him, tapping the edge of a book impatiently.
“Leonardo, you cannot spend the entire morning staring out the window!” Monsieur Duval barked. “Racine will not memorize itself. Andromaque, my boy! Begin from Act One!”
“Yes… yes, Monsieur Duval,” Leonardo replied, dipping his quill into ink.
“Ah! What is this?” Duval exclaimed, pointing to the tiny sketches and lines of writing scrawled in the margin. “You were doodling again! A Montreux writes with purpose, Leonardo, not with idle fancy!”
Leonardo flushed but did not speak.
Instead, he whispered under his breath, “Idle? These are stories… stories about real people, Monsieur Duval. About justice… and kindness.”
Duval’s eyebrows furrowed. “Do I hear disobedience? Speak clearly!”
“Non, non, Monsieur. I imagine the lives of others… while I read,” Leonardo said quickly, hoping the explanation would satisfy.
Duval looked at him with confusion. “Imagination is dangerous for a noble. Words are meant to impress, not to question the world.”
“Yes, sir,” Leo answered.
After the lesson, Leonardo retreated to the violin, where Madame Celeste, the violin and etiquette tutor, waited.
“Leonardo, again you frown as you play!” She said, “Adjust your posture! Music is a voice; your voice. Let it be heard, okay?”
“Yes, Madame Celeste,” he replied. His fingers moved the strings with precision and elegance.
Later, in the privacy of his small study, Leonardo whispered to the candlelit pages of his journal:
“If only wealth and privilege could serve the many, not just the few…”
A knock interrupted him. “Leonardo, your mother says dinner,” called a servant. He sighed but nodded.
At dinner, his mother beamed across the long table. “Leonardo, my dear, your tutors say you excel in literature and music. But you drive off into dreamland quite often. Nevertheless, such brilliance! Tell me, what grand idea occupies that clever mind of yours?”
Leonardo paused, unsure how to express himself. “I… I think about people, Mother. People outside the estate… how they live.”
She laughed softly. “You mean to say your imagination is wandering again?” Her eyes darkened. “For now, remember who you are, and put your everything into elevating the Montreux name.”
Leonardo lowered his gaze and said nothing.
“Have you heard your mother well?” his father asked sternly.
“Yes, Father. Yes, Mother,” he replied, forcing a smile as he looked at them.
He thought to himself: One day, my words will reach beyond these walls… one day.
That night, Leonardo sat by his large crystal window that opened onto a small balcony, gazing at the moon.
The moonlight refracted through the glass, scattering rainbow-like patterns that shimmered in his eyes.
“Monsieur Duval may scold me… Mother may praise the piano… Father may think only of the family name… but the streets, the children, the injustices—they will not be ignored.
I will write for them. I will find a way to make the world just.”
He opened the window and stepped onto the balcony, looking out at the faintly bustling city before returning to his chamber.
While the Montreux estate slept, Leonardo remained awake, writing by candlelight at his study table.
Life at the Montreux estate was always a matter of maintaining balance. Mornings were devoted to literature, philosophy, and French poetry, where Leonardo debated ideas with his tutors, sharpening both mind and wit.
Afternoons were piano and violin lessons, evenings for secret writing, and now, at nineteen, he needed to acquire a new skill: knightly training.
One beautiful morning, Leonardo walked into the dueling yard, where a young noble from the Ruby estate, named Rufus, waited with swords in his hand.
“Leonardo, I hope you’ve been practicing,” Rufus smirked. “I wouldn’t want to embarrass a Montreux heir today.”
Leonardo adjusted his stance. “Rufus, the Montreux name may carry weight, but skill gains it over time. I intend to earn respect, not merely claim it.”
“Oh, how intriguing. Consider me compelled by such wisdom,” Rufus said, tossing a sword to Leonardo. Leonardo caught it, and their practice began.
The sun rose with the clashing of blades. Rufus was quick, strong, and clever, while Leonardo moved with agility and strategy in every strike.
“Your footwork has improved,” Rufus admitted as he dodged a blow. “I see your tutor has been keeping you busy.”
Leonardo smirked. “Rufus, you are my tutor. What are you saying?”
“I know,” Rufus replied as Leonardo advanced. He untied his red hair, its bright crimson catching the light, and flipped it aside. “I was complimenting myself.”
Rufus’s sword slipped past Leonardo’s guard, grazing his shoulder.
“You’ll have to do better than that, Rufus,” Leonardo said, smiling. “Passion alone cannot win a duel.”
After the session, Rufus clapped him roughly on the shoulder. “You’re amazing, Leonardo, perhaps more than I expected.”
“Sir Rufus!” Leonardo winced in pain.
“Yes, Monsieur Leonardo de Leo,” Rufus teased.
Leo sighed. “Formidable, yes, but purpose must guide skill. One must be ready to defend not only honor, but justice.”
“Justice?” Rufus said, surprised. “A noble duel for justice? How charming. It seems we have a chevalier before us.”
“That is my goal,” Leonardo said calmly. “To bring justice to this land, no matter what it takes.”
“Solid conviction. I like that!” Rufus grinned, his sharp canine flashing.
“You eat too much meat,” Leonardo muttered.
“You’re not wrong,” Rufus laughed.
Neither of them noticed the circle of ladies who had been watching from the start of their sparring to its end.
“What a crowd,” Rufus said. “Ladies, the battle is over. You may return to your affairs.” The women dispersed, giggling among themselves.
That evening, Leonardo returned to the library, his hands still trembling faintly from the duel. Just as he opened his journal, a soft knock echoed through the room.
“Leonardo, may I have a moment?” said Madame Elise.
“Of course, Madame Elise,” he replied, bowing slightly.
She stepped inside, gently closing the door. “Over the years, I’ve noticed something in you—a philosopher’s spark, a thinker’s mind. Tell me, do you write?”
Leonardo hesitated. “I… I try, Madame Elise. But my father insists on appearances, on duty, on title. I must obey.”
Elise moved closer. “Obedience is wise, my dear. But obedience does not forbid thought. Think freely, and when the time is right… the world will listen to those who prepare with diligence and excellence.”
Leo nodded slowly. “I will… I will prepare, Madame Elise. I promise.”
“Thank you,” she said softly, turning to leave.
“But Madame!” he called after her. “Why show interest now, when you once scolded me for it?”
“That is because…” she paused, glancing back with a faint smile. “I am your conscience.”
Then—Leonardo awoke.
“What a dream,” he murmured, glancing at the pendulum clock. It was midnight.
“How long have I been asleep?” he wondered, rising and returning to his dormitory. There, he drifted back to sleep.
The next morning, Leonardo slipped out discreetly with a small purse of livres, wandering through the narrow streets near the Montreux estate. Children ran barefoot, merchants shouted, and the poor begged.
He spotted a boy hunched over a loaf of bread. Leonardo approached quietly. “Here,” he said, pressing a few coins into the boy’s hand. “Buy what you need… and eat well.”
“Merci, monsieur!” the boy exclaimed, eyes shining.
Later, he noticed a struggling artist painting a scene in the square. Leonardo handed him a small pouch. “Keep your colors… and your vision alive,” he said softly.
“You… you are too kind, sir, or shall I say Monsieur Leonardo?” the artist said, smirking.
“It is nothing, William,” Leonardo said, smiling at him. Both knew each other from the bread loaf incident but showing each other acquaintances remarks will bring in gossip.
“Don’t worry William, I’ll stop this segregation,” he whispered to him.
“Until then, I’ll dream for that day,” William answered back, adjusting his beret. "Just small steps toward what should be," Leo thought to himself.
That evening, Leonardo lay in bed, candle flickering, journal open, thinking:
“Why does wealth blind those who hold it? Can duty and justice coexist? What good is power if it serves only a few?”
He thought of his father then whispered, “I obey… but I cannot unsee what I have seen. I cannot ignore what is wrong.”
He yawned then blew on the candle extinguishing it. He put his journal under his bed and looked at the ceiling.
“The world is heavy,” he whispered as he drifted to sleep.
Leonardo Montreux had always carried himself with quiet grace — a gentleman in every sense.
His speeches were powerful, his manners refined, and his gaze caring. Even in a conversation, his words carried a rhythm like music, easily captivating.
One late evening, on the streets of LaCroix glistened beneath the dim light of lanterns. Leonardo had gone out in search of his butler, Xavier, who had left earlier but hadn’t returned for quite a while.
It was then that he heard a woman’s frightened cry.
Leonardo quickened his pace. Turning a corner, he saw two drunken men tugging at the sleeve of a young woman as she tried to free herself.
Without hesitation, Leonardo marched toward them, angered by such behavior.
“Enough, messieurs. Step away from her.”
The men turned, sneering. “And who are you to command us, monsieur?”
Leonardo’s posture straightened, his eyes darkened.
“A man with dignity and standards. Certainly, standards higher than yours.”
“Something in his tone feels like nobility,” one of them whispered to the other.
“Let’s go,” the other answered. They pushed the woman to him, muttering curses, as they walked down the streets.
Leonardo approached the frightened woman, offering his jacket with a gentle bow.
“Here, madame. Take this. You should not be alone at this hour.” She accepted it with trembling hands. “Merci, monsieur… I do not know how to thank you.”
“There is no debt to be paid,” Leonardo replied softly. “Only a promise — that you walk safely home.”
She nodded and went back home. Meanwhile, Leonardo continued his search, finally spotting Xavier near a spice shop, holding a lantern and looking flustered.
“Master Leonardo!” Xavier exclaimed. “What are you doing at this hour of the night?”
“I feared for you,” Leonardo smiled, “so I came looking for you.”
Together they returned to the Montreux estate. The night was quiet once more, except for Leonardo’s thoughts and inspiration.
Weeks turned into months, and Leonardo’s reputation within the Knightly Order grew swiftly. His calm intellect, discipline, and moral composure earned the respect of both the commanders and peers. Rufus of the Ruby estate would often joke, “At this rate, Montreux, you’ll have the King’s sword before any of us!”
Leonardo only smiled humbly. “A sword is only as worthy as the hand that guides it. I seek not glory, but purpose.”
Rufus clapped, smiling brightly. “I am glad I have taught you well, my friend.”
Years passed, and the boy who once watched a child eat bread in the streets had become a man whose words shaped minds.
From a young age of seven, he had grown into a twenty-three-year-old whose heart balanced wisdom and courage.
Under his own conscious guidance, through dreams and his own unwavering conviction, Leonardo compiled his thoughts into a book titled “Of Justice and the People.”
It was a work unlike any other — eloquent, reasoned, and brave. In it, Leonardo wrote:
“No kingdom stands tall upon the suffering of its base, else, the tower will crumble. Justice must exist as freely as birds do without bounds; reaching noble and peasant alike.”
The book spread swiftly among scholars, nobles, and even the common folk who could read. Copies were whispered through salons, discussed in academies, and quietly admired by reformers. Leonardo gained the title of an enlightenment thinker—a title only a few gained.
Soon, a royal messenger arrived at the Montreux estate.
“By order of His Majesty, the King requests the presence of Monsieur Leonardo de Montreux at Versailles tomorrow. His Majesty has read your work and wishes to speak with you personally,” he declared.
Leonardo’s father became silent. His mother clasped her hands, half in shock, half in pride. And Leonardo bowed his head humbly. “If it is His Majesty’s will,” he said, “then I shall present myself at court.”
That night, as he stood by his crystal window. “The pen has begun…its masterplan, its masterpiece,” he said.
The next day, Leonardo straightened up as the royal carriage drew up to the Montreux estate, and looked at Xavier, who inclined his head respectfully.
The carriage doors opened, and a royal messenger stepped forward.
“Monsieur Leonardo Montreux,”
Leonardo entered the carriage. His father, Duke Montreux, looked at his son then smiled. “He has finally elevated the name of the Montreux family.”
His mother in an ecstatic mood said, “The King! Imagine! All of Paris shall speak of you!”
“So long, mother and father,” he greeted as the royal carriage began to roll through the streets of Paris.
At the palace, Leonardo was guided into a grand, golden hall. The King’s expression softened when he saw the young man before him.
“Ah, Monsieur Montreux,” the King said, “Your writings on justice have reached my ears. Such wisdom in one so young is rare. I would like to hear more, perhaps even seek your counsel.”
Leonardo bowed gracefully. “Your Majesty, if my words can serve truth and justice, I am honored to offer them.”
The King nodded approvingly. “You may go,” he said, then murmured to himself, “Such depth in youth… a true Montreux.”
As Leonardo walked through the marble corridors, the glitter of gold meant nothing to him. What mattered was justice, a treasure far greater than wealth or fame.
When he returned home, his parents eagerly asked about the King.
As he shared the news, joy and pride filled their hearts.
That night, gazing at the lights of the cit, Leonardo understood: justice was not a crown to wear, but a burden to carry with grace.
Two years later...
Before the royal court, the King placed a sword upon his shoulder.
“I hereby grant you la Légion d’Honneur,” he declared.
Leonardo rose, “My deepest thanks, Your Majesty.” He smiled.
The audience applauded. Yet as Leonardo stood up, he knew his honor was not built on a sword alone, but on a mind guided by compassion, justice and truth.
He had reached the highest knightly order, not through power or pride, but through wisdom and courage that sought to heal the world he had sworn to protect.

 “The pen is mightier than the sword.” -Edward Bulwer-Lytton
