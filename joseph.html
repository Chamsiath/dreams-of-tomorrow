Blind Joseph 👓

One year after the Holocaust…
“Joseph, are you ready for your first day at school?” asked his mother, Sarah.
“Ima, I don’t want to go to school,” Joseph replied, tears welling up in his eyes.
Ima means ‘mother’ in Hebrew.
Sarah hugged him tightly. “Oh, my poor little honey,” she whispered. “You’re safe now. You’ll see, everything will be alright.”
Joseph Cohen, twelve years old, had come from a war-torn Germany. His family had fled after suffering unimaginable losses during the genocide caused by the Nazi regime. Joseph himself had lost his sight when a piece of shrapnel pierced his eyes. His younger sister, Abigail, and his father, Moshe, were gone. Now he lived in a small house with his mother and her brother, Benjamin.
The family’s resources were limited; three square meals a day were a luxury they could not always afford. The government had done them a “favor,” providing a small house and free education.
The classroom was buzzing with whispers when Joseph entered, his hand brushing along the wall to guide him. He felt the soft tap of the teacher’s hand on his shoulder and heard her voice:
“Class, we have a new student today. Joseph, would you like to introduce yourself?”
Joseph’s voice was soft but steady. “I’m Joseph Cohen. I’m from Germany.”
Immediately, the murmurs began. Some students whispered among themselves, glancing at his curled peyos, the traditional sidelocks that marked his faith. A few others stared at his brownish-blonde hair, confused.
“Is he… a Jew?” someone muttered.
The questions and stares made Joseph shrink slightly. He had faced so much, but the discomfort of being singled out never got easier.
Just then, a dark-skinned boy in the back of the room looked up. His name was Isma’il. He had always been quiet, often the target of subtle and not-so-subtle prejudice because of the color of his skin. Isma’il’s eyes met Joseph’s.
In that instant, Isma’il realized he wasn’t entirely alone.
Joseph went to the back of the classroom and sat beside Isma’il. Isma’il felt a flicker of shyness. He hugged his books to his chest, shrinking slightly, and for a moment, he felt exposed, no one had ever chosen to sit near him before. Slowly, he began to feel a deep unease about his own skin, comparing himself to others and valuing white-skinned individuals more than himself. His mother had been sold as a maid to the royal family and now worked in the palace; he saw her only rarely, if at all. His father, a skilled farmer, had earned the king’s favor to such an extent that the child’s tuition was covered entirely, yet the comforts of school could not shield Isma’il from the quiet ache of his own self-doubt.
“Good morning,” Joseph said, facing the opposite side.
“Um, Joseph, I am at your left,” said Isma’il shyly.
“What is your name,” he asked, this time facing him.
“My name is Isma’il,” he said shyly. “Isma’il…are you a Muslim?” he asked.
“Yes, I am a Muslim. My parents are from Ethiopia," Isma’il answered.
Joseph brought out his hand for a handshake but Isma’il didn’t touch Joseph’s hand.
“You don’t like handshakes?” Joseph questioned.
“Well, you see, I am black so…blacks touching white skin is considered insane. As though I am an untouchable,” Isma’il said as a tear fell on the table. Joseph then started touching Isma’il’s face.
“Why are you touching my face?” Isma’il questioned.
“Blind people usually touch faces to recognize the face pattern so that next time they know who they are in case of liars. Please Isma’il, stop crying. The fact that you are black, the fact that you are a Muslim or the fact that whatever is making you cry is not for the right cause. You are crying due to low self-esteem. Please do not cry, ever because of your race. Hold my hand,” said Joseph then Isma’il held Joseph’s hands.
“You see? Not all fingers are equal but each is unique and has a role. The same applies to society. Okay?” said Joseph.
“Okay,” Isma’il said.
“Um, Joseph?” said Isma’il.
“Yes?” Joseph answered.
“Are you an Arab?” Isma’il asked.
“I came from an Arab descendant so yes we can say that. But my nationality is German,” said Joseph.
“You can write even though you can’t see?” Isma’il asked.
“Yes, I can write both Braille and English,” Joseph answered.
A man rang the bell and so the first period began.
Home time…
“Joseph!” the mother called as she walked towards him.
“So how was school?” the mother asked.
“It was fine, ima,” said Joseph.
She chuckled and brought him back home.
The next day, Joseph walked into the school courtyard with a quiet sense of determination. He had memorized the layout of the school, the positions of the benches, even the texture of the gravel on the path, and yet, his heart bumped faster as he sensed the whispers before he even reached his classroom.
“Look… it’s the blind boy!” someone sneered.
“He’s a Jew, right? From Germany?” another added, their voices illed with mockery.
Joseph’s stomach tightened, but he held his head high. He could not see the faces staring at him, but he felt the hostility like sharp winds on bare skin.
As he neared the entrance, a group of boys lunged forward. Joseph’s notebooks were snatched from his bag.
“Hey! Give them back!” he called, his voice trembling.
The boys laughed cruelly, tossing his notebooks into the air. Joseph could hear the splash before he realized what had happened: his carefully written notes were sinking into the pond behind the school. He reached out, hands grasping at water and mud, but it was no use.
“Stop it! Stop!” he shouted, but the boys weren’t finished. One shoved him lightly in the back, another pushed at his shoulder. Joseph stumbled, arms flailing, and then, he fell. The cold water engulfed him instantly. His clothes clung to him, mud and leaves sticking to his hair and soaking through to his skin.
Panic and humiliation surged at once. He thrashed for a moment, but his arms weren’t enough to resist the weight of his drenched clothing. When he finally managed to pull himself up onto the bank, he collapsed, shivering, his hands pressed to his face as he began sobbing silently.
Isma’il, who had witnessed the whole scene from across the yard, ran over as fast as he dared. He was strong, yes, but he knew the consequences of retaliation, especially for a black boy. Injuring one of the boys could mean punishment far worse than the one Joseph had already endured. Still, he could not leave his friend alone.
“Joseph… Joseph, it’s okay,” Isma’il said, kneeling beside him. His voice was low, and steady. “I’m here. You’re okay. They can’t touch what’s inside you. Your kindness.”
Joseph tried to wipe the water from his eyes, but it only blurred more, mixing with the tears that had already fallen. “They… they hate me,” he whispered, his voice cracking.
“They don’t know you,” Isma’il said softly. “They only see what they fear, what they don’t understand. You… are worth more than their rudeness.”
Joseph’s hands trembled in the mud, but slowly, he reached for Isma’il’s hand. He could feel the warmth and strength there. For the first time that day, he felt anchored, as if he were not entirely alone in a world that had suddenly become cold and hostile.
“They took my notebooks… all my work…” Joseph’s voice was small and shaky.
Isma’il gently squeezed his hand. “Then I’ll help you. We’ll start again, one page at a time.”
Joseph cried even more, his tears falling freely.
“You’re right,” Joseph whispered in the cold air. “I… I won’t let them make me feel less. I can’t see them, but I can feel everything else. And that’s enough.”
The boys who hurt him had already moved on, laughing at someone else. Isma’il held Joseph’s hand as he stood up.
“You know,” Joseph said, his voice stronger now, “I’ve memorized the pages of my notebook. I remember everything. They can take the paper, but not the knowledge I carry inside me.”
“R-Really?!” Isma’il asked.
Joseph nodded.
Isma’il gasped, then started laughing with Joseph.
Joseph took a deep breath; the cold air filled his lungs. The shameful moment by the pond had left behind something new: a friend, Isma’il.
The bell rang, marking the start of class.
During the break…
“Hey, you two!” a cheerful voice called as Joseph and Isma’il opened their lunchboxes.
Joseph turned his head, recognizing the tone. A British boy, a bit taller than Joseph, with messy sandy hair and an easy smile, walked up to them.
“I’m George, a new student,” he said, holding out his hand. “I saw what happened yesterday. Those guys were terrible. You shouldn’t have to go through that alone, and… I’m sorry I didn’t help.”
Joseph hesitated, then shook George’s hand. “You don’t have to be sorry. Thank you… I really appreciate it,” he said softly.
Isma’il, standing behind Joseph, looked nervous. He played with the strap of his bag and avoided eye contact. “I… I’m Isma’il,” he said quietly.
George smiled warmly. “Nice to meet you, Isma’il!” he said, shaking his hand firmly.
“I hope to see you both soon,” George added as he walked away.
Days turned into weeks, and the bond between Isma’il and Joseph grew stronger. Eventually, George became part of their small circle, a trio full of joy and laughter.
During another break…
George sat down between them cheerfully. “What’s for lunch today, gentlemen?”
“I’m eating Shiro Wat, a pea stew,” said Isma’il shyly.
“And you, Joseph?” George asked.
Joseph touched his lunchbox, opened it, and smelled it gently. “Cholent, a stew,” he replied.
George gasped playfully. “He can tell what food it is just by smelling it! Blindness really does make the other senses stronger.”
“I brought cakes and biscuits,” said George proudly.
As he spoke, his elbow knocked his juice box over, spilling juice on the floor. “Oh no! Sorry! I’m a disaster with drinks,” he muttered, trying to clean it up.
Isma’il giggled softly, a rare sound that made Joseph smile. “At least you didn’t spill it on my stew,” Joseph said.
George grinned mischievously. “If I did, you’d call me the Juice Villain of Birmingham.”
He picked up a biscuit, held it like a microphone, and began singing dramatically, “And now… presenting the one-man school choir!”
Joseph chuckled and tapped George’s shoulder. “You’re lucky I can’t see your face, or I’d be laughing too hard.”
Isma’il laughed out loud this time, a bright, genuine laugh that filled the lunchroom.
George raised his biscuit like a trophy. “Victory! I made someone laugh!”
Joseph smiled, touching the biscuit thoughtfully. “I don’t know if it’s the biscuit or your silly singing, but… okay, you win.”
George puffed out his chest proudly. “See? That’s British charm! Give me a few months, and you’ll both be eating pudding and drinking tea like true gentlemen.”
Isma’il laughed softly. “I doubt that. You tried last week, and we nearly burned the kettle.”
George gasped. “That was an experiment! My mum says great ideas start with mistakes.”
Joseph smiled. “Then you must be the most creative person I’ve ever met.”
They all laughed together, their voices mixing warmly with the noise of the lunchroom until a harsh voice cut through.
“Hey, terrorist! Give me your English notes,” a boy sneered, walking toward Isma’il.
The laughter stopped.
Isma’il froze and quietly handed over his notes.
Joseph turned toward the sound of the bully walking away. “Leave them, Isma’il,” he said calmly. “Their rudeness has no place in a heart full of kindness.”
George’s smile faded. Joseph reached out and touched his face gently.
“Why are you touching my face?” George asked softly.
“To recognize you,” Joseph said. “So that no matter how the world changes, I’ll always remember the faces of those who stood by me.”
George smiled. “You’re strange, Joseph… but the good kind.”
Joseph tilted his head, smiling faintly. “Then maybe there’s still hope for all of us.”
Fifteen years later…
The air in Birmingham had changed. The war was long over, but its memory lived on in scars and strength. A tall building stood proudly in the city center, its sign shining:
“The Unity Foundation — Founded by Joseph Cohen & Isma’il Dawud.”
Inside, photos of their journey filled the walls, pictures of school days, laughter, tears, and protests against injustice. The foundation had become a refuge for people hurt by prejudice for their race, faith, or disability.
Joseph, now in his late twenties, walked with a cane in one hand and a notebook in the other. His beard was neatly trimmed, and though his eyes were still cloudy, they now showed peace, not pain.
Isma’il stood beside him, dressed neatly with a calm smile. “You know,” he said, “I still remember that first day you touched my face. I thought you were weird.”
Joseph chuckled. “And I thought you were too quiet for your own good.”
Just then, the door opened. “Guess who’s here!”
It was George, now older, broader, dressed like a businessman, holding a briefcase and, of course, a box of biscuits.
“I brought nostalgia,” he said, waving the box. “And some investment ideas.”
Isma’il laughed. “You and your biscuits never change.”
Joseph turned toward his voice. “George, the man with more crumbs than wisdom.”
George grinned. “Hey, those crumbs helped fund half your meetings back then!”
The three friends burst into laughter, their voices echoing like memories that never faded.
As they sat down in the meeting room, Joseph ran his fingers along the table’s edge and said, “Fifteen years ago, we were just three boys trying to understand the world. Now, maybe… we can help make it better.”
Isma’il nodded. “Not maybe. We will.”
George lifted a biscuit like a toast. “To unity, hope, and to never spilling juice again.”
They laughed once more and in that laughter was healing, friendship, and peace.

 “Where there is a will, there is a way.” -George Herbert
