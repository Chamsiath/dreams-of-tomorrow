Tara, the Environmentalist 🌿

The year was 2050. The sky over Melbourne had lost its blue color; it had become grey from smoke that never cleared. Every night, the news spoke of floods, dying crops, and children starving in distant places.
Tara sat on the couch, watching the news on TV. Her mother sighed beside her.
“Every day it’s something new,” she murmured. “The world’s burning faster than we can fix it.”
Tara looked at her, then at the small plant on the windowsill. She stood up and touched it. “Will it ever grow back, Mama?”
Her mother smiled. “Maybe, if your generation tries harder than we did. Things get worse as the years go on.”
Tara didn’t fully understand. She was only ten, but she often wondered: How can one child fix a broken world?
That night, she dreamed of forests, and a voice echoed throughout the trees: If we die, you will too.
She woke drenched in sweat but determined to help, fearing the extinction of humanity due to deforestation and pollution. The next morning, she began collecting what others threw away, filling a box she called her “treasure chest.” Behind her house, she watered a dying lemon tree with leftover dishwater until one small leaf appeared.
After school, she sat in the library, reading about rainforests and animals she had never seen. At dinner, she’d tell her mother, “Bees are disappearing,” or “Coral turns white before it dies.”
Her mother would listen and talk to her about animal extinctions.
When other kids played, Tara planted seeds in biodegradable bags. When they talked about toys, she talked about trees. They laughed at her.
“What’s the point?” they asked.
Tara just smiled and went on believing: Even one leaf matters.
Before sleeping, she wrote in her journal, the “Book of Green,” a little notebook filled with drawings of forests and animals. Beneath one sketch, she wrote, “One day, I’ll make it all grow again.”
By the time she reached high school, the world had only grown darker. The air stung her lungs, and disasters had become normal. But Tara’s fire was not extinguished. She organized her first protest in the school cafeteria during breaks. Some mocked her, others threw food at her. She cried—yes—but she didn’t let them define her.
Then one day, she met Lily — a quiet girl who sat at the back of class, drawing leaves and trapped birds. Tara leaned over and whispered, “You care about this stuff too?”
Lily looked up, surprised. “Of course I do. Humanity depends on nature, yet humans have grown selfish enough to believe nature depends on them. It is we who caused this disaster.”
Tara was struck by her maturity. “Let’s form a duo — LiTa, Lily and Tara,” she said, smiling.
“Sure,” Lily said, shaking Tara’s hand.
From that day on, they were inseparable. After school, they would walk to the small patch of coastline left untouched by factories, where the water was murky but still home to aquatic life. Every weekend, they visited different beaches to remove garbage — plastic bags, litter — and help restore the ecosystem. Sometimes, they found turtles trapped in human-made debris; sometimes they didn’t, but they continued cleaning, undeterred.
At school, Tara would talk endlessly to Lily about planting trees, restoring rivers, and raising awareness. They created posters reading: “Kill One, Plant Ten,” handing them out to classmates. Some laughed, others ignored them, and a few joined their cause.
Their friendship grew stronger each day. They shared secrets, fears, and dreams.
“One day,” Lily said softly, “we’ll change more than just these beaches.”
“We have to,” Tara replied.
Together, they planted saplings in empty lots, cleaned beaches, nurtured rooftop gardens, and documented the damage they saw. Tara realized something important: hope wasn’t just a feeling; it was action. And with Lily by her side, it felt possible.
By their late teens, the world outside had grown harsher. Streets were coated in smoke from nearby factories, and even the small coastline they had tended for years had changed — once-blue waters now carried layers of plastic and oil. Pollution in every form — air, noise, water — was everywhere.
Every weekend, Tara and Lily continued their work. They freed turtles caught in nets and pulled fish from oil-slicked water, though not all survived. One day, a baby sea turtle slipped through their fingers, carried away lifeless by the tide. Tara buried it in the sand, tears falling.
“I’m sorry,” she whispered. “We weren’t fast enough.”
Lily knelt beside her, placing a hand on her shoulder.
“Tara… we are doing what we can. That little life… it wasn’t ours to save alone.”
“But it feels like everything is falling apart, turning into a disaster. Diseases of every kind because of pollution,” Tara said with deep sorrow.
“Let’s just do what we can,” Lily said with a small smile. “We’ll leave the rest to God.”
“I just wish I could do more,” Tara murmured.
“Don’t worry, we will,” Lily said, squeezing her hand. “Until then, let’s just hope for the best.”
These moments were heavy, but they refused to stop. Their weekend missions became protests, campaigns for planting, cleaning, and educating children who didn’t know about the calamities around them. Most people ignored them, some even laughed at the sight of two girls working tirelessly. But Tara and Lily didn’t care—their goal wasn’t approval; it was change. Real change.
The news only deepened their pain. Headlines reported droughts, famines, and mass extinctions. Koalas had vanished from the wild; polar bears drifted on melting ice; black rhinos were gone forever; Arctic glaciers had disappeared. Each report landed like a punch, a reminder of how fast the Earth was dying.
One evening, Tara and Lily sat on the beach, watching the sun set.
“Sometimes I wonder if we’re just tiny ants trying to stop a flood,” Lily said.
“Maybe,” Tara replied, “but even ants can move mountains if they don’t stop.”
That night, they returned home exhausted, carrying more trash than usual. With Lily by her side, Tara realized that grief and anger could fuel action—that the devastation around them was a call to rise.
It was in these hard moments that Tara reaffirmed her resolve. She didn’t yet know how far she would go, or what she would sacrifice, but one truth was clear: inaction is betrayal.
The world outside seemed to crumble. Droughts spread across continents, famines struck families, and the extinction of animals they loved weighed on them. Koalas, polar bears, and black rhinos had disappeared, and each headline made Tara more anxious.
One evening, the girls sat on Lily’s rooftop, watching the sun sink behind the smoke that swayed across the city. The streets were never quiet—machines roared, cars honked, and voices shouted.
“It’s getting worse, isn’t it?” Tara asked, eyes fixed on the sun.
“Every day,” Lily admitted. “I didn’t think it could get this bad this fast.”
“Sometimes I feel like we’re fighting a monster we’ll never beat,” Tara said, staring at the darkened skyline.
“Then we have to strategize to beat the monster,” Lily said, placing her hand over Tara’s. “Promise me we’ll never stop. No matter what.”
“I promise,” Tara said, gripping Lily’s hand tightly. “We fight for every life that can’t fight for itself.”
That night, they mapped out plans for weekend cleanups, petitions, protests, and small tree-planting campaigns. It was a promise forged in desperation—a vow that would carry Tara through heartbreak, anger, and, eventually, sacrifice.
By the time Tara and Lily entered university, the world had become harsher. The sky was filled with smoke, factories brought out black clouds, and children wore masks as part of daily life. Homemade foods were scarce; fertilizer-fed meals dominated, intoxicating people and leading to new illnesses—new disasters.
The university offered new opportunities: environmental science classes, student organizations, and public forums where they could speak out. They organized campaigns, led seminars, and petitioned local authorities. Posters reading “Kill One, Plant Ten” decorated bulletin boards, and their quiet activism began to attract attention.
But challenges grew heavier. Industry leaders scoffed at their ideas, professors advised moderation, and many students mocked their passion.
One afternoon, as they prepared for a protest near a river thick with oil, Tara whispered to Lily, “Do you ever feel like no one’s listening?”
“All the time,” Lily admitted, adjusting her oxygen mask. “But even if only one person changes, isn’t that worth it?”
“One person isn’t enough,” Tara said, frustration showing in her eyes. “We need everyone to wake up.”
“Then we will keep waking them up,” Lily said, “Together.”
Their days became a cycle of lectures, research, and activism. The city seemed indifferent, but change was coming, even if it was as small as a sapling of hope planted in someone’s heart.
Tara often reflected on the species already lost: koalas, polar bears, black rhinos.
“How horrible,” she said, a tear rolling down her cheek.
Student groups began planting saplings, neighbors reduced waste, strangers shared the message of ‘kill one, plant ten’ online.
One quiet morning, Tara and Lily worked along the riverbank, lifting floating plastics and untangling nets from trapped fish. Lily’s cough had been persistent lately, she kept visiting the doctor and until one day—a phone call.
Lily held the screen in shaking hands.
“Tara… this… it’s bad,” she said, trembling, eyes glued to the screen.
“Bad? What do you mean?” Tara’s voice trembled as she stepped closer to Lily.
“It’s… lung cancer. Stage three,” Lily said as tears rolled down her cheeks. “They… say it’s from the pollution. All the smoke, the factories… I’m not strong enough.”
Tara staggered back as if the words themselves had struck her. “No… that can’t be. You… You're the strongest person I know! You fight every day!”
Lily smiled faintly, as she began crying more. “Strength doesn’t stop the world from poisoning itself.”
For days after the diagnosis, Tara and Lily tried to keep doing what they loved. They returned to the river, picking up trash and freeing fish, though Lily’s coughing fits grew worse with each trip. Every day, Tara watched her friend grow weaker, until she couldn’t and retreated to the hospital for further care.
One afternoon, as Tara came to visit Lily, Lily whispered, “Promise me… you’ll finish this. Even if I can’t.”
Tara’s tears fell freely, soaking Lily’s sleeves. “I promise! I swear, I’ll make them listen! I’ll make them all listen!”
The day Lily was laid to rest, Tara could barely stand. She dropped to the ground beside the coffin, hands clawing at the dirt.
“I swear… I swear I’ll stop this!” she screamed, voice cracking. “I’ll fight for every life you couldn’t save!”
It was that day, kneeling in grief and rage, that Tara’s purpose became absolute. Lily’s death, caused by a world unwilling to change, ignited a fire that would push her forward. She began planning campaigns, speeches, and protests with urgency — every action now fueled by love, sorrow, and a determination that no child should ever lose a friend to pollution again.
Even in her sorrow, Tara returned to the river each weekend, lifting debris. After Lily’s funeral, the world felt different and harder.
She went on—organizing cleanups, planting trees wherever she could, speaking at local schools. Her slogan, “Kill One, Plant Ten,” began appearing across the city. Students, neighbors, even strangers started asking, “Who is this girl fighting so fiercely?”
One afternoon, she stood on a stage she made outside a factory, speaking to a growing crowd.
“We cannot sit and watch as the world dies!” she shouted, gripping the edges of her banner. “Every tree cut, every river poisoned, every life lost is a choice we make together. I will not stop, and neither should you!”
A man in the back scoffed. “You’re just one girl. What can you do?”
Tara’s eyes flashed. “It wasn't only me!” she teared, “My friend was with me and now! —” she paused, “the smoke killed her and I am alone shouting to a group of people who are playing the deaf that they cannot listen to a message which is for their own good!” she shouted, silencing the crowd murmuring. “One girl can plant ten trees! One girl can free a turtle! One girl can make the world see what they refuse to see!”
The crowd murmured, some nodding, some uncertain. Slowly, others joined her: students with posters, volunteers with garbage bags, people planting trees. What began as a solitary act of mourning became a movement.
Tara’s voice did not stay confined to her city. With little money and hope as her only companion, she traveled across continents. She spoke in small halls in the United States, on crowded streets in Canada, and in university auditoriums in England. Each audience was different, yet the message was the same: the Earth was dying, and silence was no longer an option.
She slept on floors and in cheap hostels, often skipping meals to save enough for a bus ticket or train ride. Her hands grew calloused from handing out flyers and planting saplings wherever she went. Each trip brought stories of suffering — polluted rivers, extinct species, families struggling to survive.
By the time her campaign gained international attention, the United Nations extended an invitation for her to speak. Standing before the assembly, Tara took a deep breath.
She began formally and respectfully, “Distinguished delegates, leaders of nations, and citizens of the world, I come before you not for recognition, but as a witness to the consequences of inaction. Our forests vanish, our oceans choke, and our children breathe poisoned air while wealth continues to amass in the hands of the few.”
“I urge you to act, not for politics, not for profit, but for life itself. Every policy, every decision must prioritize the Earth and its creatures over the accumulation of wealth. If we fail now, we fail not just ourselves, but every generation to come.”
She spoke as someone who had faced grief, hardship, and injustice. When she finished, UN members were stunned into silence, some nodding, others leaning forward, deeply moved by her sincerity.
The applause was not thunderous but was light. Tara knew that speeches alone could not save the planet, but she had done something more important: she had awakened conscience.
And with that, she stepped down, fully aware that true change demanded courage and sacrifice.
The world’s oldest tree, The Heartwood, stood in California. She arrived at the forest, carrying only her banner, a small backpack, and the weight of every life she had ever mourned.
The ground shook as the bulldozers approached. Men in helmets barked orders, glaring at Tara. Tara felt scared, but she did not step back. She planted herself firmly in front of the ancient tree.
“Stop!” she yelled, “You will not cut this tree! You will not destroy what we cannot replace!”
The operators hesitated, laughter in their eyes, thinking her a lone girl, powerless. Tara raised her banner higher.
“This tree has survived storms, fires, and centuries of human indifference. You will not be the ones to silence it!”
Birds circled above as the machine lurched forward. Tara did not move.
The next moments blurred—the roar of machines, the crunch of earth, the world shrinking to a single heartbeat. Tara fell, still clutching the banner, her eyes fixed one last time on the ancient branches that had outlived countless human lives.
News of her death spread instantly. Footage of her protest went viral. People around the world watched in shock, hearts guilt at the sight of a lone girl facing machines that marched over her. No leaders wept, no politicians acted—but ordinary people did. Crowds rose in protest, voices demanding change. Small revolts sparked, then grew, fueled by grief and outrage.
A man arriving at the site noticed a bloodstained piece of paper. He picked it up and read:
“If you are reading this, then it means that the enemies of nature have succeeded in killing me but not my voice. I urge all of you: plant, revolt, stand up. Nature is dying, and if nature dies, we follow.
—Tara.”
The note shook him to the core. Tears streamed down his face as he read it again, realizing the fight she had started could not be silenced. Across continents, others found copies, shared them, and the message spread faster.
In Australia, Tara’s body was returned home. Her mother knelt beside the coffin, crying. Friends and inspired strangers planted trees around her resting place in memory of her.
Though Tara was gone, her voice continued to reach across the world. The Earth had lost a defender in flesh, but humanity had gained a symbol, a spark that no bulldozer, no machine, could ever erase.
Years passed, but Tara’s voice never faded.
In Australia, the saplings around her grave had grown into young trees, reaching toward the sky. People came from far and wide to honor her memory, planting more trees, cleaning rivers, and restoring land. Her story became a lesson.
Around the world, governments could no longer ignore the growing public outcry. Laws were passed to protect forests, rivers, and endangered species. Pollution was reduced, and environmental education became part of every school. Communities that once ignored the Earth now fought to protect it.
Tara had dreamed of joy—not for herself, but for the Earth, its creatures, and the children who would breathe clean air and drink safe water. Though her life was short, her dream lived on. Every new leaf, every freed turtle, every tree planted was a quiet victory in her name.
The Earth still bore scars, but life began to bloom again. Rivers ran clearer, forests grew taller, and endangered animals slowly returned. Humanity had learned what Tara had tried to teach: nature’s survival is their survival too.
Fifty years later…
The Earth was healing. Forests stretched farther, rivers flowed clear, and birdsong returned.
In a meadow, a little girl knelt in the soft soil, her hands pressing a young sapling into the earth. She looked up and whispered, “For Tara.”
Somewhere, it seemed as if Tara was smiling too. After so many hardships and struggles, her legacy lived on in every sapling, every caring hand, and every heart that remembered to protect the Earth.
Hope would never be silent again, and thus joy and peace began to spread across the world—simply by saving nature.

 “Little by little, one travels far.” -John Ronald Reuel Tolkien
