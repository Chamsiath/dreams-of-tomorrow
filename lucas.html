Lucas, the Fearful Child 🩺

“Lucas, are you ready?” his mother asked.
“I don’t want to go to the dentist!” Lucas shouted, stomping his foot.
“But you have to. It’s for your own good. You need braces,” his mother said as she opened the car door for him. Lucas got in reluctantly, mumbling as they drove toward the hospital.
“I hate doctors!” Lucas said, crossing his arms.
“But doctors save lives,” his mother replied calmly. “When I had complications during your birth, a doctor performed a C-section to save both your life and mine.”
“Hmph!” Lucas huffed, rolling his eyes.
Lucas was a Norwegian boy living in Manchester. He loved chocolate and hated hard candy. His mother, Elena, was a passionate Spanish woman who enjoyed spicy dishes that made him cry from the heat.
Doctors, however, were Lucas’s greatest enemy. He had never liked them, and for some reason, they never seemed to like him either.
When they arrived at the dentist’s office, Lucas clung to the door as if his life depended on it. The dentist smiled warmly while holding a shiny tray of tools.
“All right, Lucas, let’s get you that perfect smile,” she said.
“I said I hate this!” Lucas shouted, flinching at the sound of the drill in the background.
Then something unexpected happened. A small, squirming puppy from the waiting room burst in, chased by a giggling little girl. Lucas froze in confusion. The puppy tripped, tumbled, and landed right on his shoes. He blinked and then erupted into laughter.
“Look at him!” Lucas giggled. “He’s worse at walking than I am at sitting still!”
The dentist raised an eyebrow. “Maybe that’s your spirit animal,” she teased. Lucas laughed so hard he almost forgot about the braces.
As the procedure started, Lucas’s nervousness turned into small jokes. “Hey, don’t poke my tongue with that thing! I swear it bites back,” he said, making the doctor and nurses laugh.
Minutes felt like hours, but Lucas managed to stay still long enough for the braces to be placed. When the dentist finally said she was done, Lucas blinked at his shiny new braces and then grinned.
“I still don’t love doctors,” Lucas admitted, closing his eyes and crossing his arms, “but… that wasn’t so bad. And I got a puppy to laugh at!”
His mother smiled, ruffling his hair. “See? Change can feel strange at first, but sometimes it brings funny little surprises.”
Lucas looked at his reflection in the metal tray. “Okay… maybe I can survive this,” he muttered, already imagining all the funny faces he could make with his new braces.
Three years later…
“Lucas?” said the teacher.
“Yes,” Lucas replied, his voice hoarse from talking too quickly.
“Sit down, please. And Lucas…” The teacher paused, her eyes softening. “I hear you’ve been helping around town lately?”
Lucas swallowed. He had been spending evenings chasing small mysteries in the neighborhood: missing bikes, strange noises at the old library, and even a case of a cat that had disappeared for three days. Detective work had become his new passion, though it wasn’t exactly what his parents expected.
“Y-yes, ma’am,” he murmured, cheeks warming.
The teacher nodded and returned to writing on the board. Lucas sank into his chair, thinking that detective work didn’t involve scissors, syringes, or the horrors of last year’s biology class. He had nearly fainted the first time he had to look at a frog for dissection. Even the smell of formaldehyde made him shiver.
His mother had been ecstatic when he showed improvement in math and science. “Maybe you’ll grow up to be a doctor,” she said brightly.
“I don’t want to be a sadistic person,” Lucas replied.
“Lucas! Doctors are not sadistic!” his mother said.
“Their operations and injections tell me otherwise,” Lucas muttered as he walked away.
“Never will I be a doctor! Never! The thought of handling blood and organs? What a horror,” he whispered to himself, shuddering at the thought of needles.
Detective work was a safe escape.
The first case of the semester came quietly. The principal’s cat, Whiskers, had gone missing, and the school buzzed with whispers. Lucas perked up at the chance to investigate.
He grabbed a notebook, scribbled observations, and started asking questions. Unlike the doctor’s office, no one expected him to perform or risk getting hurt. He followed small clues: a scrap of red ribbon here, a paw print in the mud there.
Hours later, Lucas found Whiskers stuck in the janitor’s closet. The cat hissed and swatted at him, but he held firm, whispering, “I’ve got you, little one.” He realized he wasn’t shaking like he had in biology class.
The cat suddenly scratched his cheek.
“Aw! You little whiskers,” Lucas said.
The next morning, he returned the cat to the principal.
“Good morning, Mrs. Maples. Your cat,” he said, handing it over.
“Oh, thank you!” said the principal, hugging him.
“Ma’am, stop hugging me!” Lucas said, embarrassed.
Mrs. Maples chuckled. “So, your aunt can’t hug you anymore?”
“Not at school, ma’am… it’s embarrassing,” Lucas muttered, blushing.
She burst out laughing. “You’re afraid your classmates will make fun of you, am I right?”
As she calmed down, she noticed the scratch. “What happened to your cheek?”
“Oh, your cat scratched me,” Lucas replied, which made her laugh even harder. Feeling humiliated, Lucas stormed out of her office.
A junior saw Lucas’s red face and Mrs. Maples laughing inside. “What happened?”
“Nothing!” Lucas said, turning even redder. The junior giggled as he entered the office.
The next week, the biology teacher announced dissections: frogs, and this time, it was mandatory. Lucas froze. The same panic that had made him flee before returning. That morning, his mother had told him, “Lucas, this is part of growing up. A doctor faces all sorts of things. You’re strong enough.”
Lucas clenched his fists. “I don’t want to be a doctor,” he whispered. “I just… want to be me.”
The day of the dissection came. Lucas’s heart pounded. He hadn’t slept a wink. He looked at the frog, the scalpel, and his classmates, who were preparing confidently.
“Mr. Nilsen, you seem quite sleepy,” said his biology teacher, Ms. Elenor.
“Oh, I’m sorry…” he mumbled.
He lifted the scalpel, trembling, and then fainted on the spot.
When Lucas opened his eyes, he was lying on the nurse’s cot, fluorescent lights shining above him. The air smelled faintly of antiseptic and orange juice.
“Mr. Nilsen, are you feeling better?” asked Ms. Elenor.
“I… I think so,” Lucas whispered. “Did I faint?”
“Yes,” she said gently. “Right in the middle of class. And, Lucas…” She leaned closer. “You may have set a new school record.”
Lucas blinked. “Record?”
“For the fastest faint-to-floor maneuver,” she teased. “You went out before your classmates even realized you were holding the scalpel. Impressive coordination, if you ask me.”
Lucas groaned. He could already hear the whispers: Lucas fainted during frog dissection, again.
The nurse gave him a cup of water. “Drink. And try not to pass out again today.”
“Easy for you to say…” he muttered.
A few minutes later, Ms. Elenor returned with a mischievous smile. “I have an idea,” she said. “Since you clearly have… unique reactions to biology, why don’t you observe instead? You can be the ‘chief detective’ of frog anatomy today.”
Lucas’s eyes widened. “Detective?”
“Exactly,” she said, handing him a tiny magnifying glass and a fake moustache. “You can take notes and write what you see. No touching. Detective Lucas at work.”
He blinked and then smiled. Observation was his thing. Puzzles were his thing. But fainting? He'd try to avoid it.
By the end of class, Lucas had drawn diagrams with more detail than anyone in the room. He even added notes like: Frog has surprisingly soft toes. Eyes slightly creepy, like the principal’s cat at night.
The class burst out laughing when Ms. Elenor read the notes aloud. Even Lucas giggled.
“You’re not just facing your fears,” Ms. Elenor said. “You’re turning them into strengths.”
“Thank you, ma’am,” Lucas said with a shy smile.
That evening, he couldn’t stop talking about the day. “I didn’t touch the frog!” he told his parents excitedly. “I observed it, and wrote detective notes!”
His mother smiled. “Good. That’s progress, Lucas. Very detective-like of you.”
His father chuckled. “Just make sure your detective work doesn’t involve frogs in the living room.”
Lucas laughed. “No promises.”
Later, he began watching doctor videos, not because he liked them, but to understand what they really did.
“Doctors make people smile by curing them,” Lucas whispered to himself. “Detectives make people smile by solving cases.” He paused, then began smiling. “Finally, something in common.”
The next day in biology class, the teacher said, “Tomorrow, we’ll be dissecting frogs.”
Lucas froze. “Frogs?” he muttered. “Dead ones?”
“Yes, Lucas,” the teacher replied with a faint smile. “Dead ones. Don’t worry, they won’t bite.”
The class laughed. Lucas forced a smile. He had spent years avoiding hospitals, syringes, and anything sharp. Now, fate had brought the frog to him.
That night, Lucas couldn’t sleep. He dreamed of a frog wearing braces, hopping around and shouting, “You’re next, Lucas!” He woke up screaming, drenched in sweat.
The next morning, he thought about pretending to be sick—but that would mean going to a doctor. “Irony,” he sighed and went to class.
When it was time for the dissection, Lucas picked up the scalpel like a nervous penguin in the morning. His hands shook, but he took a deep breath and remembered his mother’s words: “Doctors save lives.”
He approached the dead frog with shaking hands. The first and second cut felt like an eternity of fear. But by the third cut, his hands became steadier and his curiosity sharper. For the first time, he found it fascinating to see how life worked inside a creature.
Weeks passed. Lucas asked more questions, and his teacher smiled. “You’ve got a scientist’s mind, Lucas.”
He smiled back. “Maybe a doctor’s mind.”
Soon his notebooks filled with anatomy sketches and doodles of doctors shaking hands with detectives. He even joined a health club though he fainted the first time he saw a blood drive.
Over time, fear became fascination. Lucas volunteered at hospitals, helped nurses, and shadowed doctors. He was changing.
After high school, he started pre-med at university. Sometimes, sitting in lecture halls filled with students in white coats and the smell of antiseptic, he wondered if he’d made the right choice. Syringes and scalpels still made him nervous.
Then, his mother fell ill. Her chest pain grew worse until the doctors said she needed surgery.
Lucas waited outside the operating room, trembling. “Please let her be fine,” he whispered. Hours later, the surgeon stepped out.
“She’s stable,” he said kindly. “The operation went well.”
Lucas jumped up, tears in his eyes. “Thank you! I don’t even know how to thank you!”
The surgeon smiled. “Then promise me you’ll take care of her.”
Lucas nodded. Later, he approached the man again. “Sir, I’m studying medicine, but sometimes I’m scared of blood, of mistakes. What if I’m not good enough?”
The surgeon chuckled softly. “Everyone’s afraid at first. Fear doesn’t make you weak, it shows that you care. The best doctors use their fear to stay careful, steady and human.”
Lucas repeated the words in his mind. Control fear... use it.
“Courage,” the surgeon added, “isn’t about not being scared.. It’s about acting right even when you’re terrified. The first time I held a scalpel, my hands were shaking. But I thought of the life before me, and my fear transformed into focus.”
He smiled faintly. “Maybe… I can do this too.”
That night, Lucas sat beside his mother, listening to the steady beeping of the heart monitor reminding him of the fragileness of life.
From then on, he studied harder. He read about anatomy, volunteered more, and practiced until he no longer froze at the sight of syringes.
One day, during a small hospital procedure, a nurse said, “You’ve got steady hands for a student.”
Lucas smiled. “Maybe fear is what keeps them steady.” he whispered, “Use fear as a barrier to surpass, not as a limit you cannot pass.”
Fourteen years later…
“Doctor Lucas? The patient’s vitals are stable,” said a nurse briskly as she walked beside him down a gleaming hospital corridor.
Lucas, now in his thirties, adjusted his surgical gloves and nodded. “Good. Let’s prepare for the bypass.”
Medical students followed him like ducklings, whispering among themselves, looking up to him in admiration. Lucas remembered being one of them once—watching from afar, full of doubt, fear, and curiosity.
As he reached the doors of the operating room, he smirked. The lights above gleamed; the instruments were lined up neatly.
He put on his mask and said, “What an intriguing operation!”
The students looked at him curiously, not understanding his reaction. But Lucas did. For the boy who once feared doctors was now one himself, the fearful child who had learned to heal not only others, but his own heart.

 “Courage is not the absence of fear, but triumph over it.” -Nelson Mandela
