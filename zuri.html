Zuri, the Weaver🧵

The Kenyan sun barely touched the red sand when Zuri woke up. Wrapped in a faded cloth her mother had sewn, she shivered in the morning breeze that came into the house. Outside, there was the smell of smoke from cooking fires; chickens clucked, and village dogs barked.
Zuri sprang up from the bed and rinsed her face from a pot.
“Time to fetch water,” she said, already knowing her daily routine. Her mother worked in the small kitchen corner, balancing a pot on the fire, while her younger siblings went to rinse their vases from a pot, sleepy-eyed.
“Zuri, hurry! The water will be hot!” her mother called tiredly.
Zuri nodded and stepped outside. She took the pot that was on the floor and headed off to the village well. At the well, she filled the pot with cool water, humming a tune her mother had taught her. “I will learn… I will do more than this,” she whispered. She asked a villager to help her put the pot on her head. The villager gladly accepted. The clay pot pressed on her as the sun continued becoming hotter and hotter. One misstep could break the pot, earn a scolding, or waste the water.
As her stomach growled, she headed back home.
The village was alive with morning activity. Women carried firewood on their heads. Men guided goats through dusty lanes. Children laughed as they ran, despite empty bellies. Zuri felt at home but dreamed of more.
Back home, she poured the water carefully into the family basin. Her younger brother tugged her sleeve. “Can we play now?” he asked.
“Not yet,” Zuri said gently. She swept the red sand floor; the dust rose into clouds which made her cough.
Her mother stirred the porridge and shook her head. “Zuri, don’t spill dust into the food again, or your father will complain about the food.”
Zuri giggled, tiptoeing around a clump of dust. “Mama, maybe Dad has secret taste buds for sand. We don’t know.”
Her mother laughed, a sound that warmed the house despite their difficult situation. “If he starts complaining, you’ll pay with extra porridge, little troublemaker!”
Even under these difficult conditions, these small moments made the day lighter. Zuri loved how her mother could make her laugh despite the situation.
The sun rose higher as Zuri continued her tasks: drawing water, sweeping, helping with food, and tending goats that wandered near the fence. Life was difficult, everyday was a battle, but Zuri felt a sense of accomplishment in whatever she did, which kept her going.
That evening, as the villagers returned to their houses at sunset, Zuri lay on the floor and whispered, “One day… I will do more. I will help those who cannot help themselves.”
Her family was a lively nest of seven children. The house smelled of dust and porridge, but also of laughter, even in hard times. Her father, young and tired, tried to keep order, while her mother moved like a whirlwind—cooking, cleaning, and scolding.
One morning, as the family crowded around the dining table, Hasani, Zuri's youngest brother took one bite and his eyes widened. “Mama… this porridge is ACTUALLY delicious today! What happened?”
Zuri burst out laughing. “Because Baba cooked it. Whenever Baba cooks, the food suddenly gets… personality.”
Their father puffed out his chest. “Exactly. Only true masters can make porridge that speaks to the heart.”
Mama narrowed her eyes sharply. “Masters? Hasani, who cooks better? Me or your father?”
Hasani froze in fear. Zuri whispered, “Choose wisely. Your life depends on this.”
Hasani pointed at Baba. “His porridge tastes… softer.”
Baba smiled in joy. Mama gasped. “Softer? SOFTER? Really? In my house?”
Zuri tried to help. “Mama, we still love yours. It’s… firm. Like a leader, determined to always do better for his people.”
Mama placed her hands on her hips. “Firm? Leader? O God, these children…”
Baba burst into laughter. “They know the truth, my love.”
Mama threw her cloth at him. The whole table burst in laughter, a laughter which ease their pain but ever hid the reality beneath it.
Food was scarce, and Zuri learned early that every grain mattered. She shared her portion with her younger siblings, keeping her own belly empty if needed.
The famine left scars. Her elder sister,Aminah, once full of mischief, grew weak and quiet before passing away. Her little brother, Baraka, followed soon after. The house felt emptier, heavier, quieter.
Yet Zuri found comfort in her best friend, Xhola. They shared many moments together—racing barefoot across the red sand, balancing water jugs on their heads.
But even friendship could not escape hardship. One day, Zuri stood frozen as Xhola’s parents—crushed by hunger and desperation—arranged a marriage for their daughter. Xhola was only thirteen, and the man waiting for her was twenty-four.
“Xhola, wait!” Zuri cried, running after her. Her vision blurred with tears she couldn’t hold back.
Xhola turned and forced a fake smile, “Don’t cry, Zuri… you’ll weave a better story for both of us someday.”
After Xhola was gone, life grew quieter. Her father, a weaver himself, gathered Zuri and her siblings and began to teach them the craft. What started as a lesson became a hobby, and weaving slowly turned into her passion.
In the middle of grief, hunger, and loss, Zuri made a silent vow: weave a different future—not only for herself, but for every girl whose dreams had been taken from her. No matter what.
One afternoon, as Zuri was weaving, her mother peeked in with a soft smile.
“Zuri,” she said teasingly, “are you weaving stories now… or mats again?”
“Both, Mama,” Zuri said, chuckling, while turning a thread around her fingers. “Maybe one day my mats will tell stories even Xhola could hear.”
One day, a white man came into the village and requested the presence of the village chief and elders. He explained how he wanted to set up a school to educate the children, and they agreed, and so the construction began.
Zuri passed the construction site whenever she wanted to draw water from the village well. Each day, the school was looking closer and closer to being fully accomplished. This made her heart jump with excitement.
“Zuri, you really love fetching water these days,” her mother remarked.
“That’s because I get to see the school construction,” Zuri beamed.
“Ah, I see. This is advantageous for both of us. You fetch the water with enthusiasm, not grumpiness, and you get to see the school construction,” her mother said.
“Yes,” Zuri nodded.
By the end of the month, the construction was done and the school started a week later. First, the boys went to school and, due to Sir Matthew’s constant urging for the girls to go to school, they soon followed.
Zuri, excited, grabbed her Kiondo bag, her pencil and notebook, and headed off to school with her brother.
It was assembly time; everyone had gathered. Zuri, Hasani and her 3 other siblings were late.
A teacher coordinated them to their respective lines.
The morning assembly was held and the principal, Sir Matthew Bradley, gave a small speech, and a teacher translated it into Swahili for those who didn’t understand English. Zuri was fixated on him. Then he noticed her and asked, “What is your name, little one?”
“My name is Zuri Kamau, and I believe education and knowledge are tools that can change humanity!” she said while smiling.
Matthew nodded. “Good. Knowledge is a tool, little one. Use it wisely. One day, you may even teach others what you learn here. You seem to be fluent in English.”
“Baba’s friend taught him when he went to study in Ethiopia. So, Baba taught us how to speak English, and Mama is from Ethiopia, so she knows how to speak it,” Zuri explained in a cute manner.
“Okay,” Matthew said, patting Zuri on the head.
“Thank you, everyone, for this wonderful assembly, and I hope to see you soon. School dismissed,” said Matthew, marking the end of the assembly.
Starting from then, Zuri’s life began to change. She weaved, did her chores, and studied. Each good grade she got made her feel closer to her goal—to weave a different, brighter story for every girl in the village.
Evenings became her favorite time and weaving her favorite hobby. At her father’s loom, she would weave patterns she had learned from him.
Her mother peeked in one evening. “Zuri, are you trying to weave the whole world into one mat now?”
“No, Mom,” said Zuri as she giggled.
Her mother laughed softly. “Just don’t forget to sleep before the sun comes back tomorrow.”
One afternoon, after lessons, Matthew stepped outside and noticed a group of children playing near the open field, laughing and running with surprising speed and skill. He approached them slowly, with curiosity.
“Excuse me… what game are you playing?” he asked awkwardly.
Zuri, helping the youngest children practice reading, paused and smiled. “This is called nyang’anyi,” she said. “It’s a game to see who is fastest and cleverest. Do you want to learn?”
Matthew laughed softly. “I think I’ll try… if you promise to play fair.”
The children giggled among themselves, then nodded.
Matthew stumbled, tripped over a stick, and landed on the red sand with a loud “Oof!” The children erupted in laughter, and even Zuri had to cover her mouth to keep from giggling too loudly.
“You see,” a child said, brushing sand off his trousers, “it’s not just about running. You have to think, anticipate where the others will move,” he said, smiling.
Another continued, “It’s like reading a story—only everyone is the letters!”
Matthew was amazed. “I see… so this is how you children train your minds and bodies while having fun. Fascinating,” he said, with his shirt now dirtied.
“Sir,” Zuri said, holding in her laughter, “your clothes.”
Matthew looked at his cloth, then sighed and said, “I am going to wash it.”
“You wash your own clothes?” a child asked, surprised.
“Yes, why are you surprised?” Matthew asked, smiling but puzzled.
The little boy tugged at Matthew’s sleeve, eyes wide with curiosity. “I thought white people have maids who do everything for them…” he said, his voice tiny and serious at the same time.
Matthew chuckled softly and brushed the child’s hair. “My boy, that’s just something people assume. Not every white person has a maid, and even those who do don’t make them do everything. Some just need a bit of help, that’s all.”
The child blinked, letting the words settle, then nodded slowly. “Okay… I understand.”
Before Matthew could say more, another child waddled over, arms stretched high like a little chick.
“Principal Matthew!” he squeaked. “Carry me, pleaaase!”
Matthew laughed, his tired face warming. “One at a time, little warrior,” he said, bending down to pick him up as the others giggled.
The moment he lifted him, another child ran over, hands in the air. “Me too! Me too!”Matthew shook his head playfully but gave in. “Alright, alright… come here,” he chuckled, lifting the next child and spinning gently as more kids gathered around, begging for piggyback rides and lifts into the air.
Zuri watched from the side, smiling, amused by how kind-hearted and open he was with all of them—no prejudice, no hesitation, just pure warmth.
Zuri just looked at them and chuckled, amused by how unprejudiced he was.
From that day on, Matthew observed the village—its games, songs, and rhythms. He realized teaching children was not just about books and numbers; it was about understanding the world and their culture and relating the most difficult concepts to the easiest of things.
Zuri watched quietly from the side, smiling. She liked that he was learning, not just teaching. “Maybe one day,” she thought, “he’ll understand why we work so hard, why we dream so big, and maybe… he’ll help us dream even bigger.”
Later, at the loom, Zuri twisted a thread as she spoke to her father.
“Bapa,” she began, “do you remember the day Matthew tried to join the children’s game?”
Her father chuckled, hands on the loom. “How could I forget? He stumbled over that stick like a newborn goat.”
Zuri laughed, covering her mouth. “He really did! But he laughed too, right there in the sand. He didn’t act like a stranger—just… humble.”
Her father smiled. “Humility is rare, little one. Even with all his learning, he does not forget to learn from others. That is what makes him a good teacher… and a good man.”
Zuri imagined him in the sand. “I like that about him, Baba. He doesn’t just teach, he watches, and listens. He tries to understand us… our games, our songs, even our silly dances.”
“Our dances are not silly. They are our culture,” the father said as he hit his daughter on the back, teasingly of course.
“Ow!” Zuri yelled out in pain.
The father sighed then said, “It seems you have learned from him too. You notice people, not only books. That’s the quality of a leader.”
Zuri smiled, looking at the patterns she wove that evening. “Maybe one day, Baba… I’ll be like that… humble and helping people dream bigger.”
Her father ruffled her hair gently. “By God’s will, little dreamer. But remember, every dream begins with small threads—just like this weaving.”
Zuri nodded.
The next morning, Matthew decided to greet the children in Swahili. He cleared his throat and said confidently, “Jina langu ni Mbuzi!”
The children froze for a moment… then burst into laughter. Zuri giggled so hard she almost dropped her notebooks.
“Principal Matthew… you just said your name is goat!” one child said, gasping in between laughter.
Matthew’s face turned fully red, and he chuckled awkwardly. “Well… that’s not quite what I meant! Let me try again…”
Before he could correct himself, a goat from the village suddenly ran into the classroom, bleating loudly and knocking over a small pile of books. Children screamed in delight, and others laughed.
Matthew ran, afraid of the goat, only to trip over a desk and land on the floor, laughing. A man, clearly from the Maasai tribe, came and directed the goat back to its herd.
Zuri and the others ran to help him up, still laughing. “It seems the goat wanted to introduce itself too, Principal Matthew!” she teased.
Matthew wiped tears of laughter from his eyes. “I suppose I need to practice my Swahili,” he admitted with a grin.
Years passed.
Years later, Zuri grew into a teenager—taller, her hands better at weaving, and her mind sharper and more determined to learn about the world.
Matthew noticed her determination. One afternoon, he saw her and said, “Zuri, you have a spark—the spark of a leader. Do you know what you want to do?”
Zuri thought of Xhola, of the girls denied education, of elders who struggled quietly. “I want to make a place where women can speak, learn, and make their own choices,” she said.
Matthew smiled. “Then learn everything you can, Zuri. Knowledge, courage, patience—these are your tools. Leadership isn’t about power; it’s about care and understanding.”
After hearing these words from Principal Matthew, Zuri studied harder, stayed up late weaving, and began teaching small groups of girls—reading, writing, and practical skills.
One day, a young student told her, “Zuri, will I ever speak to elders like you?”
Zuri smiled, patting her head. “Of course, little one. Speak with your heart, and your words will be strong.”
Her father, now older, often watched quietly. “Even threads that seem fragile can hold a strong pattern if you weave them carefully,” he said.
By her mid-twenties, Zuri’s reputation spread beyond the village. She attended workshops, met people worldwide, and her empathy and determination shaped her leadership.
Then came a letter from UN Women, inviting her to join a program supporting women’s leadership. Zuri was excited beyond words—finally, an opportunity to put her all into her work.
She traveled, spoke passionately about girls’ education, domestic violence, and community support. Humanitarian aid began flowing into East African countries, supporting programs she had imagined in her small village.
Yet no matter how far she went, her heart returned to the red sand paths. She still wove, laughed at her mother’s jokes, and carried the memory of Xhola and every girl who had inspired her.
One evening, back in her village, Zuri sat at the loom, threading colors together. Her mother looked at her.
“You’ve woven so much more than mats now, little dreamer,” her mother said softly.
Zuri laughed, remembering Matthew falling in the game years ago. “I guess humility and laughter were always part of the threads,” she said.
She truly understood the power of one determined heart. Surrounded by memories, Zuri smiled. She had made a difference—and the world, little by little, was brighter because of it.

 “Educate a girl, and you educate a nation.” -African Proverb
