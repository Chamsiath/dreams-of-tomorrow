Yuki, the Sprinter 🏃‍♀️

The sun was setting over the Tokyo athletics field, casting long shadows on the ground. Yuki could feel her heartbeat as she ran toward the finish line. The crowd seemed like a blur of colors, and the wind brushed her face as she crossed the line with a triumphant shout.
"Another gold medal," Yuki thought to herself.
"Yatta!" Yuki exclaimed as her teammates ran to her, cheering, while her coach clapped from a distance.
'Yatta' means 'I did it!' in Japanese.
At home, Yuki hung the medal next to her others in her trophy cupboard.
“One day, I’ll run for Japan,” she said, smiling to herself.
Then came the accident. A week later, her world turned upside down. Rain poured heavily that night. Her father was driving her home from training when a truck skidded into their lane. She fell from the car; everything around her seemed to tilt violently, her vision beginning to blur...
“Papa! Papa!” she screamed, her hands clawing at the ground. Her father, his head bleeding, called the ambulance. He turned to Yuki and said, “Yuki! Hold on! Help is coming!”
...the screeching tires, her father’s scream, and then darkness.
After she woke up, the doctors told her that they couldn't save her leg. Amputation—the word echoed in her mind, cold and final.
“No…” she whispered. “No, no, no!” she screamed as she began crying. Her mother hurried to her, her eyes red and puffy from crying. "Yuki, please! Listen to me. You're alive, honey. That's the only thing that matters."
"No! Without my leg, I am no longer Yuki, the student. Without my leg, I am no longer Yuki, the hero...without my leg, I am no longer Yuki, the Sprinter..." she said as she continued crying.
She refused to meet her old teammates. Her once bright room felt like a stranger’s place. When she finally returned to school, whispers followed her down the hallway.
“Is that Yuki? The track star?”
“She’s in a wheelchair now.”
“Poor thing. What a waste.”
She continued rolling on her wheelchair down the hallway, each word being like a knife piercing straight into the heart.
In P.E., her teacher, Ms. Aoi hesitated before calling attendance. “Sato-san… you can sit this one out.”
(‘Sato-san’ simply means Ms. Sato. In Japan, we use honorifics to be formal and polite.)
At lunch, a girl deliberately bumped into her chair, spilling water across Yuki’s tray. “Oops,” she said with a smirk. “Guess you can’t run from that either.”
Yuki's hands trembled but she remained silent.
That evening, she sat by the window, staring at the medals that once made her proud. They shone faintly, reminding her of her accomplishments. “What good are you now?” she whispered as a tear rolled down her cheek, falling onto her lap.
The days passed until one afternoon, when she was leaving the library, her chair got stuck on a ramp’s edge. A boy her age rushed over to help.
“Hold on, I’ve got it,” he said, kneeling to help. He pushed gently, freeing the wheel.
“Thanks,” Yuki muttered, avoiding eye contact.
“No problem. I’m Shinobu. Shinobu Yamaguchi.” he said, smiling, pointing toward himself.
She gave a polite nod. “Yuki Sato.”
He smiled. “Hm…you were on the news once, right? You are a sprinter, am I correct?”
Her chest tightened. “Was,” she replied quietly.
“Hmm,” Shinobu said thoughtfully. “I still see one sitting here, though.”
Yuki blinked. “Huh?”
He shrugged. “You might not be running now, but I can tell. You’ve still got that fire.”
“But how can a fire exist when it’s already been extinguished?” asked Yuki sadly.
“Sato-san,” he said, touching his chest lightly, “the fire here burns longer than the one in your legs.” His blue eyes gleamed.
“Good luck. See ya!” he said as he walked away.
Yuki stared after him, unsure why her heart suddenly felt lighter.
The next day, during break time, the air buzzed with chatter and laughter. Yuki wheeled herself toward her usual quiet corner near the vending machines. She put in coins and got a juice box and a packet of chips.
As she reached for her drink, two girls from her old track team passed by.
“Did you hear?” one whispered. “Coach said Yuki used to finish a hundred meters in twelve seconds. Now she can’t even cross the hallway without help.”
The other giggled. “Guess her new sport’s rolling instead of running.”
Yuki froze as a lump formed in her throat. The straw in her hand bent due to her tightening her grip on it.
They didn’t stop there. One leaned closer, “You were so lucky, Yuki. Everyone used to cheer for you. Guess fame’s easier to lose than a leg, huh?”
The laughter that followed stung more than the accident she experienced. Yuki felt angry, ashamed and helpless.
“I didn’t choose this!” she suddenly shouted, her voice breaking as tears rolled down her cheeks. The hallway went silent. “Do you think I wanted this?! To lose everything? Akari! Hana! Why are you so cruel?!”
The girls blinked, startled, then stepped closer.
“Well, well, well… it seems somebody hasn’t learned her lesson,” Akari said, gripping Yuki’s wheelchair handle.
Hana snatched Yuki’s chips and juice. Akari jerked the wheelchair backward, then shoved it forward, sending Yuki tumbling to the floor.
She hit her face against the cold tiles causing her nose to bleed as she continued crying silently. She gasped as her chair rolled away. Hana tossed a five-yen coin at her; it struck her forehead, leaving a red mark.
“Keep the change!” Hana called mockingly as their laughter echoed down the hall.
Yuki stayed on the floor for a long moment, her breath shaking, her mind turning, her dignity disappearing. For the first time since the accident, she wished she could disappear.
Then came a calm voice. “You shouldn’t let them define what you’ve already survived.”
Yuki turned. A girl who looked strikingly like Shinobu stood there, her bag slung carelessly over one shoulder, a faint smile softening her face.
“Are you Shinobu’s younger sister?” Yuki asked, wiping her tears with the back of her sleeve.
The girl nodded. “The name’s Hanae, Class 11-A.”
She grabbed Yuki’s wheelchair and helped her sit up.
“In which class are you?” asked Hanae.
“Class 10-C,” Yuki murmured.
“We’ll first give you first aid then you’ll go back to your class,” Hanae said calmly.
Hanae brought Yuki to the nurse’s office then began pushing her back toward the classroom.
“I never got your name,” Hanae said quietly.
“My name is Yuki. Yuki Sato.”
Hanae smiled faintly. “Then, Yuki Sato… don’t look down too long, the ground has nothing new to show you.”
And before Yuki could reply, Hanae turned and walked away.
For the first time in months, Yuki didn’t feel small. Her low self-esteem… began fading away.
The next morning, Yuki rolled through the school gates but for the first time in months, something small began flickering in her heart—curiosity.
As she approached her classroom, someone tapped her shoulder.
“Morning, Sato-san!” said Hanae with a grin, her brown hair tied up messily.
“Good m-morning,” Yuki replied shyly.
Hanae leaned on Yuki’s desk as though they’d known each other for years. “You know, Shinobu told me about you. Said you were the fastest person he’s ever seen.”
“He… said that?” Yuki blushed.
“Yup,” Hanae said, popping the ‘p’. “He doesn’t usually talk about people, so that’s something.”
Just then, Shinobu entered holding three cans of strawberry milk. “I thought I’d find you two together,” he said with a grin. “Here. Breakfast’s on me.”
“You brought this for me?” said Yuki, startled.
“Of course,” Shinobu said casually. “You can’t heal on an empty stomach.”
Hanae laughed. “He acts all cool, but he stayed up late designing a new ramp for the school steps. Said it was for a ‘friend.’”
“You weren’t supposed to tell her that!” Shinobu puffed.
Yuki giggled as Hanae went on teasing Shinobu.
At lunch, the three of them sat under the sakura tree in the courtyard. Petals drifted through the air. A petal landed gently on Yuki’s lap. Hanae stuck one in Yuki’s hair.
“There. Perfect.”
Yuki smiled a bright smile. “You two are weird.”
“Thank you,” Shinobu said with pride.
Hanae leaned back against the tree trunk. “You know, maybe being fast isn’t always about running. Sometimes, it’s about how quickly you find people who see you for who you are.”
“Maybe…you are right,” Yuki said, looking at the butterflies as they flew around them. Yuki stretched her hand allowing the butterfly to land on her finger as she stared at it.
“A, B, C, D, E, F, G,” Shinobu suddenly began to sing under his breath, rocking side to side.
“Seriously?” Hanae said, giving him a look. “You’re ruining the moment!”
He shrugged. “I was trying to lighten the mood.”
Yuki burst into laughter soon causing the two of them to follow. This laughter strengthened their bond all beneath the cherry blossom tree.
When the laughter finally faded, Yuki looked at them both: Shinobu’s calm and kind eyes and Hanae’s playful warmth. She began feeling something she hadn’t felt in a long time: safety.
For the first time since the accident, she didn’t feel like a broken piece of her old self. She felt like Yuki again.
As the afternoon arrived, Yuki thought to herself, “Maybe… This is what healing looks like.”
The next morning, Yuki wheeled herself into the classroom, Hanae by her side and Shinobu tagging along with a small thermos of warm tea he insisted she drink. The school smelled like fresh paper and chalk, a smell sickening to Hanae.
“I feel as though I am going to puke,” Hanae said as Shinobu and Yuki laughed.
During the morning assembly, the whole school gathered in the auditorium. The chatter slowly faded as the head girl stepped up to the microphone, her voice calm and clear.
“Good morning, everyone. This year’s Bunkasai, our school festival, will be held in the coming months. Get your presentations and performances ready and show everyone your best. Thank you.” She bowed slightly as the crowd clapped.
Then the head boy, Shinobu, took the mic. “Good morning! The Tokyo Olympics will be held on August 4th, and the Tokyo Paralympics on October 21st! Let’s work hard and make our school proud. You got this!” The applause grew louder, echoing through the hall.
Yuki’s hands gripped her wheelchair. The words “Tokyo Paralympics” made her heart flutter in a strange way. She looked at Hanae, who was smiling with the same excitement in her eyes.
As Shinobu stepped off the stage, he leaned toward Yuki. “See? That fire I talked about… still burning,” he said with a small grin.
Yuki shot him a side-eye but didn’t reply. Somehow, hearing “Tokyo Paralympics” made her chest feel lighter, like a small burden had quietly lifted.
When the assembly ended, students filled the hallways. Hanae grabbed Yuki’s hand and tugged her toward the courtyard.
“Come on, Sato-san! We have to celebrate this announcement properly!”
“Celebrate?” Yuki asked, raising an eyebrow.
“Yes!” Hanae said, waving her hands dramatically. “Snacks, chaos, and cheering you on, all at once!”
Shinobu followed behind, balancing his thermos carefully. “Let’s just not spill tea this time,” he muttered.
In the courtyard, students chatted excitedly about the festival. Yuki’s eyes wandered until she spotted a group practicing relay races. Their laughter and energy pulled at something deep inside her, something she thought she’d lost.
Hanae noticed her gaze. “You could do that too, you know. With your chair,” she teased.
Yuki blinked. “With my chair?”
Shinobu chuckled. “Of course. Wheelchair racing is real, and I bet you’d be amazing at it.”
Hanae leaned in and whispered, “Think of it as a new kind of track. Same Yuki, just a different race.”
Yuki’s lips curved into a small smile. For the first time in weeks, she felt a spark, not of running, but of hope.
“Okay,” she said softly. “Maybe… maybe I could try.”
Hanae squealed and clapped. “YES! That’s the spirit! We’ll be your cheer squad!”
Shinobu raised his thermos like a sword. “And your official hype team. Professional. Fearless and loud.”
Yuki laughed, a warm and light sound that made her heart feel like it finally had room to grow again. The Paralympics weren’t just a dream in the distance anymore. They were something she could reach, one push at a time, with her friends by her side.
As cherry blossom petals drifted softly from the trees above, Yuki thought to herself, Maybe this is the start of a new race. And this time, I won’t be running alone.
The school fair burst with colors, smells, and cheerful noise. Booths filled the courtyard, selling crepes, grilled corn, and handmade crafts, while laughter and music filled the air. Yuki wheeled herself carefully through the crowd, Hanae skipping beside her, and Shinobu balancing a bag of cotton candy.
“Slow down,” Shinobu said, dodging a flying paper airplane. “This isn’t a race, though I guess you could make it one, Sato-san.”
Hanae nudged Yuki’s arm. “Come on, let’s find the sports booth. That’s where the real fun and inspiration happen.”
At the sports booth, Ms. Aoi, their gym teacher, greeted them with a bright smile. She held a clipboard in one hand and had a whistle hanging from her neck.
“Ah, Yuki Sato,” she said warmly. “I heard about the Paralympics announcement this morning from a teacher. Excited?”
Yuki nodded, a little nervously. “Yes… I think I want to try racing again.”
Ms. Aoi’s eyes softened. “I have a younger sibling who’s handicapped,” she said. “I’ve trained with him for wheelchair and running events. If you like, I can help you figure out which path suits you better.”
Yuki tilted her head, curiosity piqued. “What do you mean?”
“Wheelchair racing,” Ms. Aoi explained, “or prosthetic running. Some athletes use specialized prosthetic blades to run. Both are incredible options; it just depends which you feel drawn to.”
Hanae clapped her hands softly. “You could totally do either, Yuki! But which one feels more like you?”
Yuki thought for a moment. She imagined herself wheeling fast down a track, but then her mind wandered to the feeling of running, her old love for the wind on her face, her muscles pumping, the thrill of crossing the finish line. Her heart clenched with longing.
“I… I want to run,” she said firmly. “I want to try prosthetic running.”
Ms. Aoi’s smile widened. “Good choice. Then I want you to meet someone, my younger brother, Takeshi. He’s trained with prosthetics before and can help you get started.”
Yuki followed Ms. Aoi around a corner to a quieter part of the fair. There, leaning casually against a track wheelchair, was a boy in his twenties. He had dark hair falling into his eyes and a small, confident smile.
“You must be Yuki,” he said, holding out his hand. “I’m Takeshi. Ms. Aoi tells me you want to try running again?”
Yuki shook his hand, a little shyly. “Yes… I… I think so.”
Takeshi grinned. “Good. Don’t worry, I’ll help you get used to the prosthetic. It’s kind of like learning to run all over again, but cooler.”
Hanae nudged Yuki. “See? Told you this would be fun!”
Shinobu, leaning against a nearby fence, whispered conspiratorially, “Cooler? That’s a high bar, Takeshi. Better not let her beat you.”
Takeshi stood abruptly from his wheelchair and lightly tapped Shinobu on the head.“It’s Takeshi-san to you!” he said, a playful sparkle in his eyes. Shinobu rubbed his head, trying not to wince.
“Sorry,” Shinobu mumbled.
Takeshi sat back down calmly. “I forgive you.”
Shinobu glared as he thought to himself: This Takahashi family! Exactly like Aoto-san, the male sports teacher, and Aoi-san. What a chaotic family!
“You just thought something insulting about my family, right?!” Takeshi barked, suddenly on the offensive.
“Err, I–” Shinobu started, but before he could finish, Takeshi dashed toward him in his wheelchair.
“Come back here!” Takeshi yelled, and Shinobu bolted down the courtyard in a mix of laughter and panic.
Yuki laughed quietly, the sound light and warm. For the first time since the accident, the spark inside her felt real and reachable. With Takeshi’s help, and Hanae and Shinobu cheering her on, maybe she really could race again—maybe even all the way to the Tokyo Paralympics.
Ms. Aoi approached, smiling warmly. “To officially participate, we need approval from the National Paralympic Committee. They’ll provide a minimum eligibility standard, and once you meet it with a few touches of training, you’re good to go. You’re young, but not too young to succeed. You got this!”
“Thank you, ma’am,” Yuki said softly.
The next weeks were a whirlwind of training, laughter, and surprisingly gentle chaos. Takeshi guided Yuki through her first steps in prosthetic running. She wobbled at first, her balance awkward, her legs unsure, but every small stride brought a spark of confidence.
“Not bad!” Takeshi called, jogging beside her. “Now faster! Imagine Shinobu chasing you, he’d never survive it!”
Hanae ran alongside, cheering. “Go, Yuki! Faster! Show him who’s boss!”
Shinobu flailed behind, puffing dramatically. “I refuse to be defeated by a girl! And a prosthetic! And… and… okay, maybe I need water!”
Each day was a mix of serious training and silly antics. Takeshi’s calm encouragement, Hanae’s relentless cheering, and Shinobu’s ridiculous dramatics turned every session into something Yuki looked forward to. The prosthetic started to feel like part of her, and with each lap, her confidence grew.
By the day of the competition, Yuki was ready—or at least she believed she was. The stadium buzzed with anticipation, bright banners fluttering in the autumn breeze. Yuki took her place at the starting line while Takeshi adjusted her prosthetic one last time.
“You got this,” he said quietly. “Remember why you’re here.”
Hanae gave her a thumbs-up from the sidelines. “We believe in you!”
The starting pistol fired, and Yuki pushed forward. Her body remembered the rhythm, the power, and the pure joy of racing as the wind rushed past her face.
With every stride, she felt the months of struggle, laughter, and determination fuse into strength. The finish line came into view, and Yuki surged, crossing it with a triumphant grin.
The announcer’s voice boomed across the stadium: “And first place, in a spectacular performance, Yuki Sato!”
The crowd erupted. Hanae squealed, racing forward to hug her. Shinobu cheered wildly, waving his arms like a possessed man. Takeshi simply smiled, calm and content, as Yuki’s heart soared.
For the first time since the accident, Yuki felt whole again—not because she had won, but because she had remembered what it felt like to chase her dreams, surrounded by friends who lifted her higher than she ever thought possible.
Hanae waved both arms like a windmill, shouting, “YUKI! YOU DID IT!” while Shinobu pretended to wipe a tear dramatically. Ms. Aoi and Aoto-san clapped proudly from the sidelines, and Takeshi gave her a thumbs-up, his grin wide and bright.
“Congratulations, Yuki!” her parents said together, smiling.
Yuki closed her eyes and let the cheers wash over her. The prosthetic felt steady beneath her, the track warm and familiar. She smiled to herself.
This wasn’t the end. It was the beginning—the start of a race that would never truly finish, as long as she kept running.

 “Strength grows in the moments when you think you can’t go on but keep going anyway.” -Anonymous
